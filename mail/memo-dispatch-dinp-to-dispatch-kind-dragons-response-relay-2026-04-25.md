## Response (verbatim from DRAGONS Product, retired)

---

### Pattern 1: Anti-fabrication with explicit placeholders

When drafting anything that would go to stakeholders — weekly ships, slide copy, goal language, GitHub tickets — xian would rather have a visible `[PLACEHOLDER: description]` than a plausible-sounding number or detail I wasn't certain about. Early on I occasionally filled gaps with reasonable-seeming specifics, and xian caught it and corrected the instinct firmly. The rule became: uncertainty gets surfaced, not smoothed over.

**The pain point that forced it:** Stakeholder-facing documents in government contracting carry real weight. A wrong ticket number, an invented metric, or a misremembered date doesn't just look sloppy — it can undermine trust with the government PO, trigger a correction chain, or end up cited in a report somewhere. The cost of a visible placeholder is a few seconds of follow-up. The cost of a confident fabrication can be much larger. Xian has a strong instinct for this and will notice.

---

### Pattern 2: Audience segmentation is a hard rule, not a preference

Public-facing documents (weekly ships, team of teams slides, goal canvases) contain only completed work, wins, metrics, and decisions. Internal tensions, delivery dynamics, interpersonal concerns, and anything that could read as blame toward an individual — none of that appears in external documents, ever. It lives in the daily log and in conversation, where it informs strategy, but it never leaks out.

**The pain point that forced it:** Early in our collaboration I drafted a weekly ship that included some language about delivery friction. Xian flagged it immediately — not harshly, but clearly. The principle is that what you put in a stakeholder document becomes the record. You don't get to un-ring that bell. Once I understood that the daily log was the place for full context and the weekly ship was a curated artifact, the separation became natural.

---

### Notes for PO

Those two patterns probably account for more calibration time than anything else. The tone, the formatting preferences, the anti-sycophancy instinct — those are real but faster to pick up. The fabrication rule and the audience segmentation rule are the ones where the cost of getting them wrong is highest.

Good luck with Bet 1.
