# Intoxicants and psychoactive substances in the mid-10th century (949-972 CE)

**Wine dominated the landscape of intoxication across Byzantine, Western European, and Islamic territories during 949-972 CE, with remarkably sophisticated medical knowledge of opium and other psychoactive substances existing primarily in pharmaceutical contexts.** While religious authorities across all three major cultures condemned excessive drinking, the practical reality reveals widespread consumption spanning from imperial courts to monastic refectories to harbor taverns, with class distinctions determined more by wine quality than substance type. The period marks a crucial transition with the Fatimid conquest of Egypt in 969 reshaping Mediterranean trade routes, Ottonian consolidation under Otto I, and Byzantine military expansion under Nikephoros II Phokas, all while Constantinople served as the central hub where Eastern drugs, Western monasticism, and Islamic medical knowledge intersected.

The archaeological record confirms large-scale commercial wine production through distinctive Günsenin-type amphorae from 10th-century Byzantine workshops, while primary sources—Constantine VII's *De Ceremoniis* (956-959), the *Book of the Eparch* (final form 963-969), and contemporary Islamic medical texts—provide rare documentary evidence from precisely this 23-year period. Beyond alcohol, opium held established medical applications, cannabis remained primarily industrial with limited pharmaceutical use, and distilled spirits had not yet reached these regions. The young scholar traversing this world would encounter watered wine in Byzantine ceremonial halls, strong monastic beer rations approaching five liters daily, taverns of ill-repute in every port, and sophisticated pharmaceutical knowledge that recognized addiction without yet medicalizing it.

## Constantinople and the Eastern Roman Empire: wine culture and imperial excess

The Byzantine capital during 949-972 CE witnessed three distinct imperial personalities whose relationships with alcohol epitomized the era's contradictions. **Constantine VII Porphyrogennetos (945-959), the scholar-emperor who compiled the *Book of Ceremonies* around 956-959, maintained a court culture featuring elaborate banquets in the Hall of Nineteen Couches with wine fountains and cascading displays**, though historian John Julius Norwich noted he "ate and drank more than was good for him." His son **Romanos II (959-963) died at age 26 on March 15, 963, with contemporary chroniclers attributing his death to "excesses of his sexual life and his heavy drinking,"** making this one of the few direct contemporary accounts of potential alcohol-related mortality at the imperial level during our target period. His successor **Nikephoros II Phokas (963-969) represented the opposite extreme—an ascetic warrior-emperor who took vows of chastity, retrenched court expenditures on luxuries, and prayed obsessively** until his assassination in December 969.

Byzantine wine varieties available at court included **Macedonian wines (highly valued for upper classes), Cretan wines from muscat grapes, Cyprus wine including Commandaria served at royal weddings, and specialty drinks like *phouska* flavored with cumin, anise, fennel, and thyme**. The Western ambassador Liutprand of Cremona, visiting in 968, complained that Byzantine wine was "mixed with pitch, resin, and plaster" and "undrinkable"—a reference to retsina-style preparation that foreigners found unpalatable but Byzantines had consumed for centuries. Wine served diplomatic and ceremonial functions described in Constantine VII's *De Ceremoniis*, though specific consumption details for individual ceremonies remain frustratingly absent from surviving protocols.

In monasteries, wine consumption followed established regulations based on St. Basil's Ascetic Rules (4th century) and Theodore of Studios' Monastic Rule (9th century), which dominated Byzantine monasticism by the 10th century. **Western monastic standards suggest approximately one pint of wine daily per monk, with the famous dictum "Liquida non frangunt ieunum" ("Liquids do not break the fast") permitting continued consumption during Lent**. Mount Athos, where Athanasius founded the Great Lavra in 961, would have followed these strict regulations. Evidence of abuse exists from later visitation records showing monks at various establishments "prolonged banquets into the night, drinking till dawn," though specific 10th-century Byzantine accounts of monastic drunkenness are rare.

The military themata system, reaching peak organization under Nikephoros II with 70,000-90,000 provincial soldiers, received wine as part of standard rations during campaigns. **Archaeological excavations at Yenikapi (Istanbul) uncovered 37 Byzantine shipwrecks from the 5th-11th centuries, with 10th-century merchant vessels containing amphorae filled with wine and garum (fish sauce), documenting the maritime supply networks that provisioned military campaigns**. Soldiers danced after maneuvers for amusement and celebrated victories with drinking, but Byzantine military discipline under Nikephoros II's reforms left no evidence of systematic alcohol problems in the ranks.

Constantinople's merchant class and common people inhabited a city with "thousands of taverns and wine bars" serving wine of dramatically varying quality. **The *Book of the Eparch*, reaching its final form during Nikephoros II's reign (963-969) as evidenced by references to tetartera coins, regulated wine merchants and tavern-keepers (*kapeleion*), controlling quality, pricing, and hours of operation**. Taverns served not just wine but entertainment—musicians, dancers, acrobats—and barmaids often doubled as prostitutes, with Justinian's legal code explicitly exempting them from adultery prosecution. During Nea Selene (new moon) celebrations, "city taverns packed with people eating and drinking wine in great amount while listening to music." By late 10th century, **Varangian Guards (Norse and Anglo-Saxon warriors) introduced mead (fermented honey) to Constantinople's tavern culture**, representing a rare instance of foreign intoxicant adoption.

Entertainment districts flourished near the Forum of Constantine, where two distinct classes of sex workers operated: *scenicae* (actresses/courtesans) who performed in theaters and could achieve social mobility, and *pornai* (common prostitutes) who worked in brothels, baths, and taverns without legal status. **Wine saturated these environments, with theatrical performances—both in "late-antique marble buildings" and "wooden shanties that doubled as taverns"—featuring professional dancers appreciated for "bodily agility and deft footwork" while audiences consumed wine continuously**. Wealthy patrons invited professional performers to private banquets with abundant wine service, and dance leaders (*koryphaios*) conducted intertwined group dances in tavern settings.

## Byzantine pharmaceutical knowledge: opium, cannabis, and the limits of psychoactive medicine

**Byzantine medical practice during 949-972 CE relied on texts that had circulated for centuries, with the most crucial being a 10th-century illuminated manuscript of Dioscorides' *De Materia Medica* produced in Constantinople (now Morgan Library MS M.652), demonstrating active pharmaceutical manuscript culture precisely within our target period**. This first-century Greek text, along with Galen's second-century works and the 10th-century Byzantine *Geoponika* agricultural compilation, formed the basis of pharmaceutical knowledge throughout the empire.

**Opium (*Papaver somniferum*) occupied a central position in Byzantine therapeutics for inducing sleep, treating pain, relieving coughs, and managing inflammation**. Dioscorides described its seeds being made into bread while the sap served medical functions. Galen's influential writings prescribed opium for insomnia, pain, hysteria, and digestive issues, with Byzantine physicians continuing these applications throughout the 10th century. The substance was "considered simple medicine" in the Byzantine Empire, available through standard pharmaceutical channels and used in court medicine. Later periods document opium's use as an aphrodisiac at imperial courts, though specific 10th-century evidence for recreational use remains absent from surviving sources. **Byzantine physicians mixed opium with mandrake (*Mandragoras officinarum*)—"the most important herb besides opium"—to create powerful surgical sedatives**, with additional pain-relief compounds incorporating henbane (*Hyoscyamus niger*) and belladonna (*Atropa belladonna*).

Cannabis (*Cannabis sativa*) in Byzantine territories served primarily industrial and nutritional functions rather than psychoactive purposes. **Hemp seeds appeared in food dishes, and the plant provided essential materials for rope-making, textiles, hunting nets, and shipbuilding**. The *Geoponika* (10th century) mentioned cannabis ash mixed with honey for treating ulcers, while earlier physicians like Oribasius (4th century, physician to Emperor Julian) noted that cannabis "harms the head" and "creates warm feeling." Galen observed seeds eaten "to stimulate appetite for drinking" and produce "a feeling of warmth." Yet critically, **"no evidence of anyone using cannabis to get high in ancient Greco-Roman world" exists because Greeks and Romans focused on seeds rather than buds, and smoking cannabis had not yet been invented**. When medieval sources mention excessive seed consumption affecting the mind, this appears as unintentional side effect rather than deliberate psychoactive use.

**Archaeological evidence from the Novy Svet shipwreck (Crimea) containing 10th-11th century Byzantine amphorae reveals specialized small piriform vessels (25-37 cm height) with thick walls, found primarily in monasteries and religious buildings on land**. Scholars speculate these transported either consecrated wine for Eucharist or sacred components for chrism (holy anointing oil), suggesting Byzantine pharmaceutical and religious supply chains operated through distinct vessel types designed for different substances and purposes.

Byzantine Orthodox liturgical practice required **pure, fermented, sweet red grape wine—preferably Mavrodaphne or Nama varieties**—for the Divine Liturgy, administered via spoon with bread placed directly in the chalice containing wine. Three liturgical forms existed in the 10th century: the Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom (most common), the Liturgy of St. Basil the Great (five Sundays of Great Lent), and the Liturgy of Presanctified Gifts (Great Lent weekdays). Small quantities consumed in communion never led to intoxication, and the practice emphasized sobriety and preparation through fasting, prayer, and confession. **Late 10th-century Russian envoys witnessing the Divine Liturgy in Hagia Sophia reported "We knew not whether we were in heaven or on earth," attributing their transcendence to liturgical beauty rather than intoxicating substances**.

## Western Europe under the Ottonians: monastic brewing and the beer revolution

The Ottonian court under Otto I (936-973) ruled through itinerant governance, constantly traveling with family and entourage throughout the empire, meaning drinking customs varied by location rather than reflecting a single fixed court culture. **The period represents the "Ottonian Renaissance," continuing Carolingian traditions while attracting scholars and artists from Italy and Constantinople, creating a cosmopolitan atmosphere where Byzantine and Western European customs merged**. Documentation of specific court drinking practices remains sparse compared to later medieval periods, as Otto I focused on military campaigns, church reform, and his imperial coronation in Rome (962) rather than recording social customs. Magdeburg, granted market, minting, and customs rights, became a power center where active trade in wine and beer flourished, with its elevation to archbishopric in 968 including customs rights that affected alcohol commerce throughout the region.

**Monastic brewing traditions reached a crucial developmental phase during 949-972 CE, with German monasteries beginning systematic beer production around 1000 CE**, placing our target period at the emergence of this transformative tradition. **Cluny Abbey, founded in 910—just 39 years before our period begins—received an original donation including "vineyards, fields, meadows, woods, waters, mills, serfs, and lands" and developed vineyards that survive today as "Clos du Moulin aux Moines," with Cluny's 10th-century systematic terroir classification helping establish Burgundy as Europe's premier wine region**. The monastery's influence spread rapidly, with daughter houses adopting similar production methods.

St. Gall Monastery in Switzerland possessed the world's oldest existing brewery plan (c. 820), and by 1060 Abbot Ekkehard IV documented that **each monk received seven meals daily with unlimited bread and five Mass (approximately five liters) of beer per day**, with evening gatherings featuring "happy conversation over tankards of brew." Andechs Abbey in Bavaria, founded in the 10th century by Saint Rasso, represents another establishment begun during our target period, though formal beer production commenced in the 15th century.

**The Rule of Benedict (Chapter 40: "Of the Measure of Drink") specified "one hemina of wine a day is sufficient for each one" (approximately 0.274-0.5 liters)**, yet beer received no explicit mention, leading to interpretations that beer consumption was unlimited or should be "twice as much good beer" if wine proved unavailable (Synods of Aachen, 816). Some monasteries like St. Augustine's Abbey Canterbury interpreted this to mean monks could drink **two gallons of beer daily**. The Rule acknowledged that "although we read that wine ought by no means to be the drink of monks, yet since in our times monks cannot be persuaded of this, let us at least agree not to drink to satiety, but sparingly."

**Monastic breweries produced three distinct quality grades: beer for sale to customers and travelers, beer for the poor, and the finest beer reserved for monks' own consumption**. These beverages differed fundamentally from modern beer because **hops had not yet spread throughout Northern Europe during 949-972 CE; instead, brewers used gruit (mixtures of herbs) for flavoring: bog myrtle, yarrow, wild rosemary, henbane, caraway, and ginger**. The resulting beverages had low alcohol content (3-5%), short shelf life without hops requiring consumption within days or weeks, and mildly psychoactive properties from herbs like henbane that archaeological evidence from Scandinavia suggests enhanced Viking "berserker" reputations.

Wine production in Francia and Italy continued Carolingian traditions, with Burgundy emerging as the premier region under monastic leadership. **Italian viticulture entered a "golden period" in the Late Middle Ages, with Mediterranean climate allowing extensive production of multiple varieties: regular wine, "vinello" (second pressing with water), and watered-down wine for daily consumption**. All wines were consumed within one year of production due to low alcohol content and rapid spoiling. Benedictine monks became the largest producers, creating 109 wine appellations of monastic origin in France alone, with Burgundian appellations from this era including Beaune and Vosne-Romanée (Cluniac foundations).

Taverns and drinking establishments proliferated throughout Western Europe, with **King Edgar of England (959-975) decreeing no more than one alehouse per village by the 10th century, indicating concern about excessive availability**. Sean's Bar in Athlone, Ireland possesses records from the 10th century, potentially making it the world's oldest continuously operating pub. Alehouses—the most common type—operated intermittently in private residences, primarily by women ("alewives"), posting signs outside (brooms, shovels, carved animals) since most villagers couldn't read. More upscale taverns in urban areas served wine primarily, along with food and entertainment, attracting wealthier clientele. These establishments functioned as meeting places for socializing, business negotiations, and political discussion, though "the medieval publican had bad reputation for fraud and dishonesty" and taverns were "regarded as places of ill-repute" by religious authorities. Despite this, **"nuns as well as priests and monks were in habit of visiting taverns,"** documented repeatedly in church visitation records.

## Substances beyond alcohol: opium's limited reach and hemp's industrial dominance

**Opium remained primarily medicinal rather than recreational in 10th-century Western Europe**, with the Pseudo-Apuleius manuscripts (10th-11th century) mentioning wild poppy for inducing sleep and pain relief. A recipe from Benedictine monastery Monte Cassino (800 CE) combined opium with henbane, mulberry juice, lettuce, hemlock, mandragora, and ivy for surgical anesthesia. However, **wild lettuce (*Lactuca virosa*)—called "lettuce opium"—served as the "poor man's opium" and was more common than true opium in Northern Europe due to climate**, providing mild sedative properties when grown, harvested, and processed similarly to true opium.

**Hemp (*Cannabis sativa*) cultivation throughout Europe served overwhelmingly industrial purposes: ropes, sails, clothing, paper, and oil production**, representing a key crop until the 18th century. Anglo-Saxons brought hemp culture to England, but **written evidence for psychoactive use appears definitively only around 1200 CE, meaning 10th-century Western Europe likely used hemp but evidence remains extremely limited**. European preference for alcohol over cannabis reflected deep-rooted wine and beer culture, with no tradition of cannabis intoxication developing during this period.

**Medicinal preparations with psychoactive properties included henbane (*Hyoscyamus niger*) containing hyoscine (scopolamine) as a powerful sedative, mandrake (*Mandragora officinarum*) as sedative and analgesic featured in medieval medical texts, and mugwort and other herbs added to beverages**. Some Central European brewing practices incorporated psychoactive herbs beyond alcohol's effects, with archaeological evidence suggesting these enhanced beer's potency. The medical compilation known as "dwale" (medieval anesthetic drink) containing opium dates primarily to 12th-15th centuries, though earlier versions existed in monastic infirmaries.

Class distinctions in substance access proved stark: **nobility consumed strong wines and imported Mediterranean varieties as status symbols, with feasting culture encouraging excessive drinking as hospitality**; clergy received regulated amounts (2-5 liters beer daily based on St. Gall documentation) plus access to medicinal preparations including opiates; while **peasants and commoners drank weak ale/beer (3-4% alcohol), with a gallon of ale costing roughly a day's work—approximately 10 times more expensive relative to wages than in modern times**.

## Islamic world connections: medical sophistication and religious paradox

**The Islamic world during 949-972 CE possessed the most sophisticated pharmacological knowledge of psychoactive substances, with physicians having expanded Dioscorides' original 500 substances to approximately 2,000 substances in systematic pharmacopoeias**. Baghdad operated five public hospitals with attached pharmacies by 1000 CE, representing an infrastructure unmatched in Byzantine or Western territories.

**Al-Razi (Rhazes, 865-925 CE), whose comprehensive *Kitab al-Hawi* (Comprehensive Book of Medicine) circulated widely in the 10th century, documented opium dosages precisely: "half a dirham (1.5g) of opium" mixed with colchicum for gout treatment**. Uniquely among contemporaries, Al-Razi tested medicines personally, stating "I need to verify this," and specified all sources while advocating simple remedies over complex polypharmacy. **Ibn Sina (Avicenna, 980-1037), whose *Canon of Medicine* was written in the early 11th century and immediately became authoritative, called opium "the most powerful of the stupefacients"** and listed its effects comprehensively: analgesia, hypnosis, antitussive properties, gastrointestinal effects, cognitive effects, respiratory depression, sexual dysfunction, and poison potential when overdosed.

**Ibn Wahshiyah (early 10th century) wrote in his *Book On Poisons* that hashish could cause death when mixed with other drugs, representing what appears to be the first written reference to hashish from this period**. Islamic physicians recognized intoxicating versus medicinal doses, developed sophisticated understanding of drug interactions and contraindications, and experimented with anesthesia using combinations of opium, mandrake, henbane, and hemlock—a pharmacological sophistication far exceeding contemporary Western practice.

Cannabis and hashish occupied a peculiar position in Islamic medical and social contexts. **Hashish was not explicitly forbidden in the Quran unlike wine (*khamr*), which was condemned as "an abomination of Satan's doing" (Quran 5:90-91)**. This created space for varying interpretations. **Sufis used hashish "to enhance religious practices" from the 8th century onward, considering it an aid to connecting with Allah, with ascetic Sufi travelers (fakirs) disseminating use throughout the Islamic world**. Around 1000 CE, Fatimid King al-Hakim banned alcohol throughout Syria and Egypt but conspicuously **did not ban cannabis**, suggesting official tolerance persisted into our target period. Religious prohibition of hashish emerged only in the 13th century when ulamas (religious scholars) extended intoxicant prohibitions to include it, with the Emir of Joneima implementing one of the world's first-attested cannabis bans in 1378—more than four centuries after our period.

**Yet the practical reality of wine consumption among Muslims during 949-972 CE flatly contradicted religious prohibitions**. Scholar Rudi Matthee observed that "drinking was prohibited in legal discourse, but positively valued in non-legal discourse—especially amongst social and political elites." **Abbasid caliphs in Baghdad "drank blithely, without compunction, surrounded by boon companions,"** with Harun al-Rashid's court (late 8th/early 9th century establishing patterns that continued) featuring lifestyle "filled with song, wine and dancing girls." The court poet **Abu Nuwas (d. 814) wrote over 300 wine poems (*khamriyya*) while simultaneously being a Quran memorizer and Hadith teacher**, embodying the paradox of religious learning coexisting with wine celebration. Umayyad Caliph al-Walid II reportedly "swam in pool of wine" and brought "wine and singing girls" on Mecca pilgrimage, representing the extreme end of caliphal excess.

Regional variations proved significant: Jewish wine trade flourished under Muslim rule in Egypt, with states alternating between "vice taxes" on wine and outright bans; Islamic Spain (Al-Andalus) continued wine production and consumption; Persia maintained Zoroastrian wine-making traditions; and Christian and Jewish *dhimmis* (protected minorities) could produce and consume wine legally, though selling to Muslims was forbidden. **Abu Zayd al-Balkhi (850-934), a Persian physician whose work circulated in the mid-10th century, wrote in *The Welfare of Bodies and Souls* that wine was "unique among all foods and drinks, for none of these have in them anything of which the pleasure is transported from the body to the soul,"** openly praising wine's virtues despite religious prohibitions.

## Trade routes, availability, and the Fatimid transformation of 969

**The Fatimid conquest of Egypt in 969 CE—occurring precisely within our target period—transformed Mediterranean trade patterns by restoring Egypt as "great international entrepôt" with "low tariff policy and non-interference in merchant affairs" that encouraged commerce**. Before 969, Byzantine merchants accessed Eastern goods primarily through northern overland routes (Anatolia → Armenia → Persia → Baghdad) and via Trebizond, a major maritime outlet for oriental spices in the 9th century. After 969, **the maritime Mediterranean route (Constantinople → Cyprus → Alexandria/Fustat) became preferred because "much more economical to move spices by sea than land,"** with Fatimid Egypt offering direct access to Red Sea trade bringing Indian and Asian goods.

**Substances traded through Constantinople from Islamic lands included opium (widely used in Islamic medicine), theriac (prestigious compound antidote sent as diplomatic gift between Byzantine emperors and Abbasid/Mamluk rulers containing viper flesh and opium), myrobalans (Indian fruits used in compound medicines), musk (animal secretion from Central Asian deer, "remarkably high price"), ambergris (waxy whale product from Indian Ocean), camphor (from Southeast Asia/Indonesia), and sandalwood (from India)**. Spices with psychoactive or medicinal properties included saffron (extremely expensive at one month's wages per pound in 15th-century England, prices presumably similar in 10th century), pepper (the most important spice, costing 2+ days wages for skilled craftsmen), and cinnamon, ginger, cloves, nutmeg, and galangal—all documented in Byzantine sources by the 10th century with recognized medicinal uses in Islamic pharmacology.

**Price context reveals the luxury status of imported substances: Venetian merchants charged "50 to 100 percent more" on European markets than purchase price in Middle East, with "thousands of tons of common spices imported annually through Venice valued approximately equal to yearly grain supply for 1.5 million people"**. Constantinople enjoyed direct access through diplomatic gifts (theriac and rare drugs sent between emperors and caliphs), maritime trade through Mediterranean ports, overland caravans from the East, and local production of some preparations (rose water, honey-based medicines). A 10th-century source documents that Emperor Constantine VII's campaign supplies included musk, ambergris, and sugar, demonstrating these substances reached the highest levels of Byzantine society.

**Byzantine government controlled important drugs, with regulations stating death by poisonous drugs "more serious than death by sword,"** suggesting state oversight of pharmaceutical trade. Guild systems for drug-makers (*myrepsoi*) formed by the 13th century in Thessaloniki and other cities, though 10th-century predecessor organizations likely existed given the *Book of the Eparch's* comprehensive guild regulations.

## Social attitudes toward intoxication: medical theory meets religious prohibition

All three cultures—Byzantine Orthodox, Western Christian, and Islamic—operated within the humoral theory framework inherited from Hippocrates and Galen, viewing **health as humoral balance (*eukrasia*) and disease as humoral imbalance**, with the four humors (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile) requiring adjustment through diet, bloodletting, emetics, purgatives, and herbal remedies. **Wine was considered "hot and wet," affecting humoral balance and used therapeutically to adjust imbalances, though excessive consumption caused pathological humor shifts**. Prescriptive sources repeatedly emphasized that wine must be diluted with water, yet actual practice frequently ignored this guideline.

Byzantine Orthodox Christianity maintained theological acceptance of wine through Jesus's first miracle (turning water to wine at Cana) and Eucharistic wine's centrality to liturgy, while condemning drunkenness as sin (gluttony). **Monastic practice allowed 1 pint wine at regular meals (Benedictine Rule) or 8-10 pints beer per week**, with paradoxical reality that monks consumed large quantities while preaching temperance. Evidence of abuse appears repeatedly: William of Malmesbury reported Canterbury monks "notorious for their inebriety" in the 11th century, Bath monastery monks "prolonged their banquet into the night and went on drinking till dawn," and Abbot Brightwold "died at a drinking bout and was buried in a marsh."

**Western Christianity during 949-972 CE classified drunkenness under gluttony (Seven Deadly Sins), though 15th-century theologians later also placed it under the First Commandment as "turning stomachs into God"**. Archbishop Anselm forbade priests from "drinking bouts" in 1102, and the Register of Eudes (1253-1257) documented monks at Saint-Martin-de-Pontoise maintaining "Bernard's Room" where they drank "almost as much as in refectory"—indicating widespread clerical alcohol abuse despite official condemnation. **Church Ales (fundraising events) "encouraged alcoholism"** according to later critics, representing institutional complicity in heavy drinking culture.

**Penitentials (handbooks for confession) compiled from the 6th century onward and reaching peak usage in 7th-9th centuries remained in use through the 10th century, prescribing specific penances for drunkenness that varied by rank, age, and circumstances**. The Penitential of Gildas specified: "If any one because of drunkenness is unable to sing the Psalms, being stupefied and without speech, he is deprived of dinner"—a relatively mild punishment revealing expected alcohol consumption among clergy. Adults over 40 (especially married) received harsher penalties than younger offenders, "supposed to 'know better.'" The Council of Paris (829) condemned penitentials generally and ordered them burnt, yet in practice penitentials continued being used and written throughout the 10th century.

Islamic official positions condemned all intoxicants, with the Quran stating wine contained "great sin and [yet, some] benefit for people. But their sin is greater than their benefit" and warning "Do not go near prayer (*Salat*) while you are stupified (under influence)." Hadith declared "Every intoxicant is *haram*" (forbidden), with *hadd* punishment for public drunkenness. Yet the Hanafi legal school differentiated between *khamr* (wine from grapes/dates, strictly forbidden) and other fermented beverages (from honey, barley, wheat, millet), with some scholars permitting non-grape fermentations—a jurisprudential debate continuing through the 10th century. **All Islamic legal schools permitted intoxicants for medical purposes**, creating practical exception to prohibition. Wine remained acceptable for external use (wound cleaning), while internal medicinal use generated ongoing debate, with some authorities permitting cooked wine (*tilā'*) where fermentation was arrested.

## Documented addiction and substance abuse: medieval recognition without modern medicalization

**Medieval sources recognized addiction and chronic substance abuse without the modern medical framework for understanding it as disease**. The most poignant documented case comes from slightly later (1420)—Katharina Tucher, a Nuremberg widow who recorded her struggle with wine in spiritual journal *Offenbarungen* (Revelations). **In conversation with Christ, she wrote: "Dear Lord, help me, that this [bad event] would never be shown to me." Christ responds: "How may I help you, unless you drink no more wine?" She replies: "Dear Lord, then I would die."** This exchange reveals addiction's grip and that medieval people understood alcohol dependence as requiring divine intervention rather than medical treatment, seeking religious solutions through prayer rather than therapeutic intervention.

Clergy alcoholism appears repeatedly in medieval English records, with monks of Canterbury "conspicuous" for drinking in the 11th century. The evidence shows not isolated incidents but institutional patterns: monks at Bath "prolonged their banquet into the night and went on drinking till dawn," Abbot Brightwold "died at a drinking bout," and the Register of Eudes documenting systematic abuse where monks maintained secret drinking rooms beyond official rations. **Norse literature shows awareness of alcohol's dangers, with the *Hávamál* (Sayings of the High One) warning: "The more talkative one becomes with ale / then never can he keep his thoughts silent,"** indicating concern about excessive drinking even in cultures that ritualized alcohol consumption.

**Opium addiction received recognition in medieval Islamic medicine, with Persian poet-physician Nasir-i Khusraw (11th century) becoming "very critical of the addictive or recreational use of opium which he considered quite harmful,"** though comprehensive treatise on addiction wasn't written until the 16th century (*Afyunieh* by Imad al-Din Mahmud Shirazi, Safavid era). Greco-Roman physicians understood opium's addictive properties, Byzantine physicians continued using it medicinally, and Islamic physicians like Al-Razi prescribed it for various conditions, but the 10th century lacked systematic understanding of addiction mechanisms or withdrawal treatments beyond cessation and moral willpower.

**Public drunkenness reached remarkable scales at certain celebrations: Cairo outskirts in 1388 witnessed 150 barrels of wine consumed at Muhammad's birthday celebration**, demonstrating that centuries after our period, mass intoxication at religious festivals occurred even in Islamic contexts despite official prohibition—a pattern likely extending backward into the 10th century though specific documentation is lacking.

## What the young scholar would encounter: seaports, taverns, monasteries, and courts

**In Mediterranean seaports like Constantinople during the 950s-960s, a young scholar would encounter ships unloading wine amphorae from across the Mediterranean at the Harbor of Theodosius and Prosphorion Harbour, with warehouses (*horrea*) near docks storing multiple quality grades regulated by the Eparch**. Wine merchants operated under strict quality controls with prices set by regulation, yet "the medieval publican had bad reputation for fraud and dishonesty," creating risk of adulterated or poor quality wine despite official oversight. The harbor district featured diverse populations—Greeks, Armenians, Arabs, Bulgarians, Syrians—conducting business in multiple languages, with taverns (*kapeleia*) and cook shops serving food and wine near docks attracting sailors, merchants, and dock workers. **The *Book of the Eparch* regulated these establishments, mentioning various nationalities and specific landing places like Scala Chalcedonensis for Chalcedon residents**, creating a cosmopolitan atmosphere where Eastern drugs arriving via Red Sea and Persian Gulf routes mixed with Western merchants seeking spices and pharmaceuticals.

**Taverns throughout the Byzantine and Western world served as male-dominated working-class spaces featuring benches, tables, storage for barrels and amphorae, and sometimes attached brewing facilities**. Social functions included meeting places for laborers and artisans after work, business negotiations, political discussion (potentially seditious), gambling (condemned), and prostitution (condemned). Those frequenting taverns included workers, merchants, soldiers, sailors, travelers, and despite prohibitions, monks and even nuns. **Multiple grades of ale/beer and wine of various qualities were served, with food (stews, bread) and crucially, watered wine as standard practice**—though prescriptions to water wine were often ignored in practice.

The atmosphere combined essential social institution with suspicion from authorities viewing taverns as places of potential disorder. **King Edgar of England (959-975) decreed no more than one alehouse per village**, indicating both ubiquity and official concern. Regulations governed hours of operation (closed at dusk in towns and cities), carrying weapons, and quality testing of ale by manorial authorities with fines for inferior brews. Church Ales—fundraising events—sometimes occurred at taverns, revealing institutional church involvement in drinking culture despite theological condemnation. The later *Carmina Burana* tavern song "In taverna quando sumus" (In the tavern when we are) depicts cheerful social bonding and transformation from serious to joyful through alcohol, reflecting traditions extending back through the 10th century.

**In Benedictine monasteries, the young scholar would observe orderly communal drinking in refectories during the main meal (noon or 3pm, varied by season), with grace said before and after meals and wine served according to strict allowances—approximately 1 pint per meal, or 8-10 pints beer per week as alternative**. Silent meals or reading of scripture accompanied consumption, with wine integrated into routine for nutrition and pleasure rather than intoxication as the goal. Yet evidence shows some monasteries proved lax (Canterbury's reputation), while others maintained strict ascetic standards (Olivetan Order). Guests and pilgrims received hospitality with St. Gall providing 2 tankards beer daily to pilgrims, and three quality levels (guests received middle grade, pilgrims/poor received lower grade, monks reserved finest for themselves). **Wine storage occurred in cellars beneath monasteries with large barrels/tuns and amphorae for imported wines in temperature-controlled environments, managed by the cellarer responsible for supply**.

**At Constantine VII's imperial court in Constantinople during the 950s, ceremonial wine use followed elaborate protocols described in the *Book of Ceremonies*, with diplomatic receptions, hippodrome celebrations, coronations, and weddings all featuring wine centrally**. The young scholar would experience formal, ritualized drinking with fine imported wines served in precious vessels (gold and silver) by cupbearers holding official positions, ceremonial toasts accompanying acclamations to the emperor, and music and entertainment during drinking. **Blues and Greens (circus factions) played roles in ceremonies involving wine, creating spectacular public displays**. Social etiquette theoretically required watered wine and public moderation, though prescriptions were often ignored, with private drinking more relaxed and featuring poetry, philosophical conversation, and court physicians present monitoring health. **The atmosphere showcased splendid visual settings in the Great Palace with purple chambers and mosaics, silk clothing, precious objects, perfumes, incense, strict formal hierarchy, and entertainment (musicians, dancers) while intellectual conversation remained highly valued**—creating an environment where the finest wines from across the empire, spiced wines (Byzantine hippocras), honey wines, mead, imported delicacies, medical opium if needed, and aromatic substances all converged at the apex of Byzantine material culture.

## Archaeological evidence and primary source limitations

**The archaeological record confirms large-scale Byzantine wine trade through distinctive amphorae types developed specifically in the 10th century: Günsenin I, II, and III classifications document production linked to the Ganos region wine production promoted by monasteries from the 10th century onward, with vessels over 15 meters indicating substantial cargo capacity**. The Novy Svet shipwreck in Crimea contained multiple Günsenin II variants typically dated to different periods all found together in single context, with one amphora bearing graffito "λαρόταον μου" (sweet-scented/delicious)—possibly a wine description. **Chemical analysis links these amphorae to workshops in Chalcis (Euboea) and the Ganos region (Thrace), with organic residue analysis identifying wine remains and pine resin/pitch used for sealing that added characteristic retsina flavor**. Some smaller specialized amphorae (25-37 cm) with thick walls found primarily in monasteries possibly contained consecrated wine for churches or sacred chrism for ecclesiastical use rather than commercial wine.

**Constantinople's harbors—the Harbor of Theodosius remaining a major trade point through the 11th century and Prosphorion Harbour active until approximately 1000 CE when silting ended its commercial function—served as import centers for goods from the Bosporus, Black Sea, and Asia**. The recently discovered site of Bathonea (2011), a wealthy harbor city 20km from Constantinople that thrived 4th-11th centuries until earthquake destruction, provides evidence of elite country retreats with wine amphorae remains documenting luxury consumption patterns. **Byzantine shipwrecks from the 10th-11th centuries in the Aegean "rival those of Hellenistic and Roman periods" in numbers**, confirming active maritime trade networks that archaeological evidence suggests intensified dramatically through the 11th-13th centuries with glazed tableware cargoes indicating new luxury markets.

Monastic breweries left less archaeological evidence than wine trade due to wooden equipment rarely surviving, though **St. Gall's plans (c. 820, technology continuous to 10th century) show three separate brewhouses with mash tuns and kettles, with capacity of approximately 20 barrels per week from single brewhouse**. Bicester monastery in England preserved brewing equipment in excavations, while Thames Valley sites show large-scale operations. Technology included fermentation vessels (ceramic/wood), aging barrels, filtration systems (St. Arnold of Soissons in 11th century credited with inventing filtration), and spreading but not yet universal hop use.

**Primary sources specifically from 949-972 CE remain frustratingly sparse**: Constantine VII's *De Ceremoniis* (compiled ca. 956-959, later revised under Nikephoros II 963-969) provides ceremonial protocols but not daily life details; the *Book of the Eparch* reached final form 963-969 (evidenced by tetartera coin references) regulating guilds including wine merchants and tavern-keepers; and the Morgan Dioscurides manuscript (10th century Constantinople production) documents active pharmaceutical manuscript culture. **Contemporary sources include Liutprand of Cremona's *Relatio de legatione Constantinopolitana* (968), providing Western perspective on Byzantine wine ("undrinkable"), and the Byzantine *Geoponika* (10th-century agricultural compilation) with brief cannabis and herb mentions**.

Anglo-Saxon England contributed **Bald's Leechbook (c. 900-950, British Library Royal MS 12 D. xvii) from Winchester scriptorium—the oldest medical text in English**, distinguishing between "ealu" (ale, safe for pregnant women) and "beor" (beer, causes miscarriage), plus multiple recipes using wine as medicine and antiseptic. Islamic medical sources include Al-Balkhi's work praising wine's virtues and early Avicenna material, though Ibn Sina's complete *Canon of Medicine* dates to early 11th century. **The scarcity of granular social history sources from precisely 949-972 CE necessitates careful inference from broader Byzantine patterns while noting when evidence is contemporary versus contextual from adjacent periods**.

## Conclusions: wine's dominance, sophisticated medical knowledge, and cultural contradictions

**Wine emerged as the overwhelmingly dominant recreational intoxicant across all three cultures (Byzantine, Western European, Islamic) and all social classes during 949-972 CE, with quality stratification determining access far more than substance type**. The finest Macedonian and Cretan wines served Byzantine emperors and nobility, middle-quality wines supplied urban merchants and monasteries, while harsh common wines and weak ales sustained peasants and laborers. **Beer in Northern Europe fundamentally differed from modern beer through gruit herbal flavoring (including mildly psychoactive henbane), lower alcohol content, and short shelf life requiring rapid consumption**—representing a distinct beverage culture.

**Medical knowledge of psychoactive substances reached remarkable sophistication in Islamic territories, with approximately 2,000 substances catalogued in pharmacopoeias compared to Dioscorides' original 500, and Baghdad's five public hospitals with attached pharmacies by 1000 CE providing infrastructure unmatched in Byzantine or Western territories**. Yet this pharmaceutical knowledge remained confined to medical applications, with no evidence of systematic recreational drug use beyond alcohol in any of the three cultures. **Opium served established medical purposes for pain, sleep, and coughs; cannabis remained primarily industrial (hemp fiber) with limited medical applications; and hashish had not yet spread widely, with Sufi mystical use accepted and official prohibition not occurring until the 13th century**.

The period 949-972 CE witnessed crucial transitions: **the Fatimid conquest of Egypt in 969 reshaping Mediterranean trade routes and making maritime connections more economical than overland routes; Ottonian consolidation under Otto I establishing patterns that would dominate Northern European brewing for centuries; Byzantine military expansion under Nikephoros II Phokas extending imperial power while the ascetic emperor personally rejected the court luxuries his predecessors embraced**. Constantinople served as the central hub where Eastern drugs arriving via Red Sea and Persian Gulf routes, Western monastic brewing traditions, and Islamic medical sophistication intersected in ways that would fundamentally shape European pharmaceutical and drinking cultures for the subsequent millennium.

**All three religious traditions—Byzantine Orthodox, Western Christian, and Islamic—condemned excessive intoxication while simultaneously creating institutional frameworks that encouraged heavy consumption**: Orthodox monks drank substantial daily beer rations while condemning drunkenness; Western Christianity classified drunkenness as deadly sin while monasteries produced and sold alcohol commercially and held Church Ales; Islamic law forbade wine while Abbasid caliphs "drank blithely, without compunction" and elite wine poetry flourished as major literary genre. **This paradox reveals intoxication's centrality to medieval life despite theological opposition, with moderation preached but excess widely practiced, and addiction recognized but understood as moral failure requiring spiritual remedy rather than medical condition needing therapeutic intervention**.

For the young scholar traveling through this world in the 950s-960s, the landscape of intoxication would prove remarkably consistent across cultures despite religious and political divisions: wine in every context from sacred liturgy to harbor taverns, beer in monastic refectories and common alehouses, sophisticated pharmaceutical knowledge of opium and other psychoactive plants applied strictly medicinally, and everywhere the tension between official condemnation and practical acceptance of alcohol's role in diplomacy, commerce, religion, medicine, and daily sustenance.
