# The Vengeful Bishop: Liutprand of Cremona's Two Faces

**Liutprand of Cremona stands as the 10th century's most vivid, vindictive, and valuable witness**—a bishop-diplomat whose bitter pen transformed personal humiliation into perhaps the most graphic writing to survive from his era. His contrasting accounts of Byzantine Constantinople reveal not just a city, but a man whose worldview pivoted 180 degrees between admiration (949) and contempt (968), offering modern writers a protagonist of contradictions: learned yet crude, sophisticated yet prejudiced, diplomatically brilliant in the West yet spectacularly undiplomatic in the East.

Born around 920 to a distinguished Lombard family with diplomatic traditions, Liutprand began as a child singer at the royal court in Pavia, leveraging his "wonderful voice" into a career that would take him from page boy to chancellor to bishop to imperial envoy. His three major works—*Antapodosis* ("Revenge"), *Historia Ottonis*, and *Relatio de Legatione Constantinopolitana*—survive as indispensable sources for 10th-century European history, though scholars universally acknowledge him as "a vivid writer and a biassed reporter." What makes him invaluable to historical fiction is precisely what makes him problematic as a historian: his turbulent temperament, acerbic wit, personal grudges, and willingness to deploy literary weapons against his enemies created texts bursting with personality, scandal, and human detail.

This dossier synthesizes primary source analysis, biographical research, scholarly historiography, and character evidence to provide a comprehensive portrait of a man who "left behind personae, not personality"—someone acutely aware that written words could become tools of self-invention, political propaganda, and literary revenge.

## A personality forged in resentment and brilliance

Liutprand's character emerges most vividly through the sharp contradictions scholars have identified. He possessed what contemporaries called a **"turbulent temperament"** marked by pride, envy (which he openly admitted), and an extraordinary capacity for holding grudges. The very title of his magnum opus, *Antapodosis*, announces his core motivation: retribution against those who wronged him, particularly King Berengar II and Queen Willa who had caused his interests to "take a hit" after his 949 return from Constantinople.

His humor operated at multiple registers simultaneously. **He was a master of Menippean satire**, mixing prose and verse in the tradition of Boethius, oscillating between tragic horror and comic absurdity. His insults achieved legendary status: calling Emperor Nikephoros II Phokas "a monstrosity of a man, a dwarf, fat-headed and with tiny mole's eyes; disgusting with his short, broad, thick beard... in color an Ethiopian; one whom it would not be pleasant to meet in the middle of the night." When Byzantine officials confiscated his purple textile purchases, he sarcastically informed them that back home "whores and conjurers wear purple"—a spectacular failure of diplomatic tact that may have doomed his mission.

Yet this same man demonstrated **sophisticated classical learning**, peppering his texts with quotations from Virgil, Horace, Terence, Cicero, Juvenal, Persius, Ovid, and Martial. He composed original Latin poetry in various meters—hexameters, elegiacs, anapaests—and became one of the first Westerners to use Greek minuscule script. His bilingual writing mixed Greek and Latin seamlessly, writing Byzantine technical terms in Greek letters, then providing transliteration and Latin translation, displaying erudition while also showing off.

Scholar Paolo Squatriti identifies Liutprand's key distinction: **awareness of "inner" versus "outer" persona**. Unlike contemporaries who wrote unselfconsciously, Liutprand understood he was crafting literary characters of himself—the vengeful chronicler in *Antapodosis*, the loyal propagandist in *Historia Ottonis*, the outraged ambassador in *Relatio*. This self-dramatization makes him ideal for fiction: he already thought in theatrical terms, casting himself as Job-like sufferer, Stoic philosopher, or cultural warrior depending on rhetorical needs.

His prejudices operated strategically rather than mindlessly. **His attacks on powerful women**—calling Empress Theodora a "shameless whore," excoriating Queen Willa—represented calculated political strategy to delegitimize rival dynasties through "genealogical doubts to further his patrons' cause." His anti-Byzantine stereotyping intensified dramatically between 949 and 968, suggesting these attitudes served political purposes rather than fixed beliefs. Similarly, his food complaints encoded cultural politics: Germanic meat-eating represented strength and masculinity, while Mediterranean vegetables, olive oil, and garum signaled weakness and effeminacy.

## From court singer to imperial propagandist

Liutprand's life trajectory reads like a medieval thriller of shifting allegiances and political survival. **Born circa 920 into a prominent Lombard family in Pavia**, both his father (927) and stepfather (942) had served as ambassadors to Constantinople, establishing diplomatic connections that would prove crucial. At age eleven (931), he entered service as a page to King Hugh of Arles, who maintained court at Pavia as King of Italy. There, educated at the palace school, young Liutprand distinguished himself through vocal talent, receiving what he called a "sound education."

He became a cleric and deacon at Pavia Cathedral while maintaining court connections. When **Berengar II, Marquess of Ivrea, gained control of Italy around 947**, Liutprand rose rapidly to become his confidential secretary and chancellor. This positioned him for his first diplomatic triumph: the 949 embassy to Constantinople at approximately age 29. Berengar's notorious parsimony meant Liutprand brought no proper state gifts, forcing him to present his own talents—and four "de luxe eunuchs" (fully castrated, the premium variety)—as if from his master.

The break with Berengar occurred around 950, shortly after Liutprand's return. **The specific cause remains murky in sources**, but timing suggests connection to Lothair's death and Berengar's consolidation of full royal power. Berengar "installed those who supported his insurrection in key positions, and marginalised or suborned those who had supported the Bosonids" (the dynasty of Liutprand's former patron Hugh). The quarrel was severe enough that Liutprand would spend years writing *Antapodosis* specifically "with the object of avenging himself upon Berengar and Willa his queen."

Around 955, **Liutprand fled to the German court of Otto I**, who "joyfully took Liutprand into his service, as a most useful agent in carrying out his plans regarding Italy." Otto recognized Liutprand's value: deep knowledge of Italian politics, Byzantine diplomatic experience, literacy in Greek (extraordinarily rare in the West), writing skills, and personal grievances against Otto's rival Berengar. In 956, at Otto's court in Frankfurt, Liutprand met Bishop Recemund of Elvira—a Mozarabic Christian serving as ambassador from the Umayyad Caliph of Córdoba—who encouraged him to write a history. Liutprand began *Antapodosis* in 958, dedicating it to Recemund, though public business repeatedly interrupted composition until 962.

**When Otto invaded Italy and became King of Lombardy in 961, he rewarded Liutprand's service by appointing him Bishop of Cremona** (formal investiture 961-962). After receiving the imperial crown in Rome on February 2, 962, Otto deployed Liutprand on critical missions: as ambassador to Pope John XII at the beginning of their quarrel (963), as participant in the Synod of Rome that deposed John XII (November 963), and as imperial envoy presiding over the election of Pope John XIII (965). Liutprand's *Historia Ottonis* (960-964) served as semi-official propaganda justifying Ottonian intervention in papal politics.

His career peaked with the 968 embassy to Constantinople, now as a senior bishop and major political figure. **After that mission's humiliating failure, sources confirm he continued "much in evidence" at Otto's court and in his diocese**. He carried letters to a synod at Milan in autumn 969, and appears in Cremona records from April 970. He possibly participated in the successful 971 embassy that fetched the bride eventually married to Otto II, though this remains uncertain. **He died in 972**—more precisely, probably before July 20, certainly before March 5, 973, when his successor as Bishop of Cremona appears in records.

## Two embassies, two worlds: transformation from admiration to contempt

The dramatic contrast between Liutprand's 949 and 968 visits to Constantinople illuminates how personal circumstance, imperial personality, and geopolitical context can transform diplomatic relationships—and provides fiction writers with a character arc built into historical record.

### The golden reception of 949: Constantine VII's hospitality

At approximately **29 years old, Chancellor Liutprand arrived in Constantinople on a goodwill mission** from Berengar II, partly to complete his Greek education (Berengar promoted this to get Liutprand's stepfather to pay mission costs). The Byzantine emperor, **Constantine VII Porphyrogenitus (r. 945-959)**, was a scholar-emperor—"a writer, scholar, compiler, collector, bibliophile, painter and patron" of the arts, known for "constant good humor" and perfect manners. Having spent most of his life overshadowed by co-emperors and regents, Constantine finally ruled alone from 945, bringing a gentle, cultured temperament better suited to books than battlefield.

**The reception was spectacular.** Liutprand was carried into the audience hall on the shoulders of eunuchs, witnessed the famous mechanical marvels (bronze tree with gilded birds singing different songs, throne that rose into the air, roaring bronze lions), and attended lavish ceremonies where Constantine personally distributed gold coins to officials. When Liutprand made an impudent remark comparing himself to Lazarus watching Dives (Constantine) enjoy wealth, the emperor good-humoredly presented him with "a large cloak and a pound of gold coins."

**Liutprand's account in *Antapodosis*** (though it breaks off mid-description) glowed with admiration for Constantinople's wonders and Byzantine culture. He learned Greek, absorbed Byzantine knowledge, and may have provided material for Chapter 26 of Constantine's *De Administrando Imperio*. Politically, **no territorial conflicts existed** between Berengar's limited Italian kingdom and Byzantium. Constantine pursued "active diplomatic relationships with foreign courts," and young Liutprand posed no ideological threat to Byzantine imperial claims.

### The bitter humiliation of 968: Nikephoros Phokas's deliberate insults

Nineteen years later, **approximately 48-year-old Bishop Liutprand arrived on June 4, 968, representing Holy Roman Emperor Otto I** on a high-stakes mission: arrange marriage between Otto II and Byzantine princess Anna Porphyrogenita, secure Byzantine recognition of Otto's imperial title, and resolve territorial disputes in southern Italy. Everything about the context had changed.

**The Byzantine emperor, Nikephoros II Phokas (r. 963-969)**, was a military commander known as "Pale Death of the Saracens" who had conquered Crete, Cyprus, Cilicia, and much of Syria. Scholars describe him as having "absolutely no social graces," a "narrow and suspicious mind," intensely militaristic temperament, and ascetic personality bordering on puritanical. Taciturn, suspicious even of closest advisers, he preferred monastic-military life to court politics. He had seized the throne in 963 and married Empress Theophano (widow of previous Emperor Romanos II), creating political insecurity.

**The reception was deliberately, systematically humiliating.** Liutprand and his entourage were forced to wait outside Constantinople's walls in pouring rain, then forbidden to enter on horseback—a public degradation. They were housed in what Liutprand called a "detestable dwelling place" that "neither kept out the cold nor afforded shelter from the heat," with no water provided and armed guards preventing movement. At banquets, Liutprand sat "fifteenth from him and without a tablecloth"—the seating arrangement itself a diplomatic message about his ranking below fourteen others.

**The food became a battlefield of cultural contempt.** Meals were "fairly foul and disgusting, washed down with oil after the fashion of drunkards and moistened also with an exceedingly bad fish liquor [garum]." Wine "tasted of pitch, resin and gypsum" (retsinated wine). A goat arrived "deliciously [sarcastically] stuffed with garlic, onions and leeks; steeped in fish sauce." For Liutprand, this represented more than bad cuisine—it encoded civilizational conflict. Germanic meat-eating signified strength and masculinity, while Mediterranean vegetables, olive oil, and fish sauce represented weakness. Nikephoros was described as "parcus" (lacking appetite, greedy of vegetables) versus Otto as "numquam parcus" (big eater) who loved meat.

**Political confrontations were equally bitter.** Byzantines refused to recognize Otto's imperial title, calling him "rega" (king) not "basileus" (emperor). When Pope John XIII's letter addressed Nikephoros merely as "emperor of the Greeks," it triggered fury—implying he wasn't the real Roman emperor. Nikephoros declared: "You are not Romans, but Lombards." Religious arguments erupted, with Liutprand declaring "All the heresies have emanated from you" and Nikephoros attacking the shallowness of Western Christianity as a "worn-out garment."

After four months, **the mission failed completely**. No marriage was arranged, no imperial recognition granted, territorial disputes unresolved. When Liutprand attempted to leave with purchased purple textiles, they were confiscated (imperial monopoly). His response—telling officials that back home "whores and conjurers" wear purple—exemplified what scholar Constanze Schummer identified as his questionable diplomatic competence in the East. After walking out of one banquet, being "despised, rejected and scorned," Liutprand fell seriously ill from "boundless depression." Upon departure, he scrawled a bitter poem on his prison-house walls: "Trust not the Greeks; they live but to betray; / Nor heed their promises, whatever they say."

### Why such radically different treatment?

**Three structural factors explain the transformation.** First, **imperial competition became existential** by 968. In 949, Berengar posed no challenge to Byzantine imperial ideology. By 968, Otto claimed to be THE Roman Emperor—direct challenge to Byzantine self-understanding as sole legitimate heirs of Rome. Byzantine imperial theory could not accommodate two emperors; this was ideological, not merely political.

Second, **territorial conflict turned active.** In 949, no Italian territory was disputed. By 968, Otto controlled formerly Byzantine-claimed territories in northern Italy and was expanding into Byzantine southern Italian possessions. Military clashes had occurred at Bari. Otto had gained fealty of Pandulf of Capua-Benevento, in the Byzantine sphere of influence.

Third, **the papal letter triggered specific retaliation.** Pope John XIII's missive addressing Nikephoros as merely "emperor of the Greeks"—widely seen as orchestrated by Otto—was a calculated insult. Nikephoros used Liutprand's reception as non-verbal diplomatic retaliation, weaponizing court protocol to assert Byzantine dominance and contempt.

**But personality differences mattered enormously.** Constantine VII's generous, cultured, secure temperament contrasted starkly with Nikephoros's militaristic, ascetic, insecure, and tactless character. Constantine could tolerate impudence with humor; Nikephoros saw only threats. **Liutprand himself had changed too**—from impressionable 29-year-old grateful for opportunities to proud 48-year-old bishop expecting respect commensurate with rank. His "candid account makes clear that often he was not as diplomatic as he might have been."

The failed 968 embassy exemplifies how personality, pride, and cultural incomprehension can doom diplomacy even when mutual interest exists. Ironically, after Nikephoros was murdered in December 969, the new Emperor John I Tzimiskes invited Latin envoys back in 971. That mission succeeded: Theophano married Otto II in April 972, bringing Byzantine recognition of Otto's title and territorial compromise. **Had Nikephoros been more flexible, or Liutprand more diplomatic, the alliance achieved in 972 might have occurred four years earlier.**

## Literary weaponry: how Liutprand wrote revenge

Liutprand's three major works represent different rhetorical modes deployed for distinct political purposes, yet all showcase his extraordinary literary craft. Modern translations and critical editions make these texts readily accessible, with Paolo Squatriti's 2007 *Complete Works of Liudprand of Cremona* (Catholic University of America Press) representing current scholarly standard, and F.A. Wright's 1930 translation available free on Internet Archive.

### Antapodosis: six books of vengeance

**Composed 958-962 but left incomplete**, *Antapodosis* ("Revenge" or "Retribution") spans six books covering European history from 888-950. Dedicated to Bishop Recemund of Elvira (the Mozarabic diplomat serving Córdoba's Umayyad Caliph), it was compiled "with the object of avenging himself upon Berengar and Willa his queen." The title announces its nature: literary retribution against those who wronged him.

**Stylistically, it's wildly inconsistent**—initially sober, giving way to "violent invective" as Berengar and Willa dominate the narrative. Liutprand mixes sacred and scandalous stories without chronological rigor, preferring entertaining anecdotes to documentary evidence. As one scholar noted, he "had a soul above documents" and was "singularly retentive of amorous scandal however devoid of probability." The work is three times longer than his other two combined, sprawling and digressive, yet containing invaluable eyewitness testimony from 935 onward based on personal experience.

**It breaks off mid-sentence** during his 949 embassy description, suggesting either loss of interest (revenge achieved through writing?) or interruption by other duties. The incomplete nature itself reveals character: even literary revenge couldn't fully satisfy his turbulent temperament.

### Historia Ottonis: propaganda as panegyric

**Covering 960-964**, *Historia Ottonis* served as semi-official apologia for Ottonian policy, focusing on Otto I's clashes with Roman nobility and papal politics. The centerpiece is the deposition of Pope John XII at the Synod of Rome (963), for which Liutprand provides the only connected narrative. He depicts John XII as sexually debauched gambler who invoked pagan gods and mutilated enemies—arranged for the pope to die "struck by devil while in bed with another man's wife" as divine retribution.

This work exemplifies **Liutprand as court propagandist**, constructing narratives to legitimize his patron's controversial interventions. Based partly on documents, it demonstrates how he deployed rhetorical skill to justify political necessity. Scholars recognize it as partisan but valuable for details no other source preserves.

### Relatio de legatione Constantinopolitana: literary nuclear revenge

**The account of his 968-969 mission** represents "perhaps the most graphic and lively piece of writing which has come down to us from the 10th century." It's a vitriolic polemic calculated to incite Otto to war against Byzantium, transforming diplomatic failure into literary triumph through sheer rhetorical force.

**The famous description of Nikephoros Phokas** achieves legendary grotesque status: "A monstrosity of a man, a pygmy, fat-headed and like a mole as to the smallness of his eyes; disgusting with his short, broad, thick, and half hoary beard; disgraced by a neck an inch long; very bristly through the length and thickness of his hair; in color an Ethiopian... with extensive belly, lean of loin... clad in a garment costly but too old, and foul-smelling and faded through age." He continues: "The old corpse... miserable burnt-out coal; old woman in your walk, wood-devil in your look; clodhopper, haunter of bryers, goat-footed, horned, double-limbed; bristly, wild, rough, barbarian, harsh, hairy, a rebel, a Cappadocian!"

**Food complaints reach satirical heights**: "The meal was fairly foul and disgusting, washed down with oil after the fashion of drunkards... a fat goat deliciously stuffed with garlic, onions and leeks; steeped in fish sauce." Cultural stereotyping pervades: Greeks are "long-haired, tunic-wearing, long-sleeved, hooded, lying, fraudulent, merciless, fox-like, haughty, falsely humble, cheap, greedy, eating garlic, onions, and leeks, drinking bathwater."

### Literary techniques and stylistic signatures

Liutprand's **genre-bending Menippean satire** mixed prose and verse throughout. *Antapodosis* alone contains fourteen original poems in various meters—hexameters, elegiacs, anapaests, asclepiads, hendecasyllabics—following traditions of Varro, Petronius, and Boethius. He oscillated between tragic horror and comic absurdity, using "kitchen humor" about bodily functions, explicit sexual descriptions rare in medieval writing, and focus on "lower bodily stratum"—genitals, sex, eating, vomit.

His **bilingual showing-off** reached spectacular levels. He wrote Greek words in Greek letters, then provided Roman transliteration, then Latin translation: "ἀδελφοί μου, adelfi mi, quod interpretatur: nequaquam, fratres, nequaquam." He deployed technical Byzantine terms (logothetes, chelandia, carzimasia) to demonstrate expertise, and substituted decorative Greek for Latin ("philargyria" for "avaritia") to display learning.

**Classical quotations saturated his texts**—Virgil most frequently, but also Horace (Odes quoted in full), Terence (assuming readers knew his comedies), Cicero ("Tullian eloquence"), Juvenal, Persius, Ovid, Martial, Lucan. Notably, he never quoted Caesar, Livy, or Tacitus. This reveals his education: court school rhetoric rather than historical training, poetry over prose historiography.

**His narrative techniques included** breaking into his own narrative to address readers directly, creating dramatic dialogue scenes, using "impossibility topos" (claiming things too horrible to describe, then describing them), breaking into verse at emotional peaks, and comparing contemporary events to classical civil wars. He self-dramatized as Job-like sufferer, new Stoic philosopher, cultural warrior.

Scholars describe his style as "chameleon-like changes"—from sober chronicle to savage invective, from theological solemnity to scatological humor, from panegyric to polemic. As F.A. Wright observed: "The Style is the man, and the triple influence of personality, environment and natural genius is plainly evident in Liudprand's writings."

## Wrestling with a problematic witness: scholarly debates across centuries

Liutprand's paradoxical status—simultaneously invaluable and unreliable—has shaped scholarly debates for centuries. The consensus remains constant even as analytical frameworks evolve: he's "a vivid writer and a biassed reporter" whose works, "in spite of his prejudices, are of exceptional value to historians."

### Nineteenth-century foundations: documenting bias

Early German scholarship established critical editions through Monumenta Germaniae Historica: G.H. Pertz (1840), Ernst Dümmler (1877), culminating in Joseph Becker's 1915 edition described as "excellent example of German scholarship." These editors recognized Liutprand's partisan nature while valuing his unique testimony. The Catholic Encyclopedia (early 20th century) crystallized emerging consensus: "Liutprand's writings are a very important historical source for the tenth century; he is ever a strong partisan and is frequently unfair towards his adversaries."

**Previté-Orton's influential Cambridge Medieval History assessment** characterized him as "amusing, relatively informative, and not entirely trustworthy," complaining that Liutprand "had a soul above documents" and was "singularly retentive of amorous scandal however devoid of probability." This established the pattern: acknowledge value while cataloging unreliability.

### Mid-twentieth century: literary criticism and disappointment

Literary critics like **Erich Auerbach found Liutprand problematic** compared to contemporaries. Auerbach praised Rather of Verona at Liutprand's expense, finding both full of "scurrilitas, indiscretion, and immoderation," but viewing Liutprand's as springing from "rancor and self-importance" rather than sincerity. Georg Misch's psychological approach devoted only 9 pages to Liutprand versus 141 to Rather, finding Rather more psychologically interesting.

**F.A. Wright's 1930 English translation**, reprinted by John Julius Norwich (1993), made Liutprand accessible to English readers but used archaic "forsooth language" that obscured his sophisticated rhetoric. This shaped Anglophone perceptions for decades—entertaining but antiquated, useful but crude.

### Late twentieth century: recognizing sophistication

The 1980s-90s brought more sophisticated analytical frameworks. **Ernst Karpf's *Herrscherlegitimation und Reichsbegriff* (1985)** analyzed Liutprand as skilled Ottonian propagandist rather than simple partisan. Jon M. Sutherland's comprehensive 1988 study provided detailed contextualization. Scholars increasingly recognized his works as carefully constructed propaganda serving specific political purposes.

**Karl Leyser's 1985 "Liudprand of Cremona, Preacher and Homilist"** revealed unexpected theological sophistication when the Homilia Paschalis (Easter sermon) was discovered, complicating simplistic characterizations. Ross Balzaretti's essay on "Liutprand's Sense of Humour" (2002) explored how he deployed humor as political weapon, using Mikhail Bakhtin's frameworks on grotesque realism and carnival.

### Twenty-first century: personae over personality

**Paolo Chiesa's 1998 critical edition** (Corpus Christianorum 156) and his subsequent articles in *Filologia mediolatina* represent current textual scholarship, superseding all previous editions. **Paolo Squatriti's 2007 translation and extensive introduction** synthesized twenty-plus years of scholarship in English, Italian, and German, arguing crucially that Liutprand "left behind personae, not personality"—he created literary characters of himself rather than revealing authentic self. This reframing emphasizes his "acute awareness of how written words could become the best means of self-presentation, or even self-invention."

**Recent scholarship (2000-2025) explores multiple dimensions**:

- **Gender and sexuality studies**: Antoni Grabowski's "From Castration to Misogyny" (2015, *Acta Poloniae Historica*) challenges simplistic readings, arguing his sexual politics require contextual understanding of 10th-century society as strategic delegitimization rather than pure misogyny.

- **Diplomatic and ceremonial studies**: Analysis of Byzantine ceremonial space, non-verbal communication, and how court protocol weaponized power display. Andrea Maraschi's work examines food culture and identity markers.

- **Historiographical influence**: How Liutprand shaped Ottonian dynastic mythology, influenced later medieval historical writing, and created enduring Western stereotypes of "treacherous Greeks" that contributed to the Great Schism (1054) and Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople (1204).

- **Literary analysis**: Recognition of his multiple personae, classical rhetorical models, Menippean satire traditions, and Boethian influences on Fortune/fate discussions.

### Byzantine perspectives and reliability questions

Modern Byzantine scholars recognize **Liutprand's profound influence on Western perceptions**: his "caricature of the treacherous Greek...stuck in the imagination of Westerners for centuries," shaping East-West misunderstanding for a millennium. Henry Chadwick's analysis in *East and West: The Making of a Rift in the Church* (2003) examines theological disputes during the embassy.

While **no direct Byzantine rebuttal survives**, comparative analysis with Byzantine sources reveals mixed accuracy. Leo Diaconus's account of Nikephoros Phokas confirms basics of his physical appearance (short, dark, military bearing) but without Liutprand's grotesque exaggeration. Byzantine ceremonial texts corroborate his descriptions of automata and court rituals. His account of Constantine VII's generosity and scholarly interests matches Byzantine sources.

**Constanze Schummer raised provocative questions** about his diplomatic competence: "How good a diplomat he really was in Constantinople, despite successes in the West?" Some scholars speculate Otto I never saw the *Relatio* or received accurate accounts of Liutprand's provocative behavior. His undiplomatic insults (purple textile incident, walking out of banquet) may have doomed the mission.

### Consensus on historiographical value

Despite all caveats, **scholarly consensus affirms his indispensability**: "No other tenth-century writer had such privileged access to the high spheres of power." His eyewitness testimony from 935 onward, linguistic knowledge (Greek literacy extraordinarily rare for Westerners), detailed descriptions of Byzantine court, and inside information on papal politics make him irreplaceable.

**The methodological shift recognizes** that his biases and distortions themselves reveal important historical information about Ottonian ideology, Byzantine-Western relations, 10th-century political culture, and how texts construct meaning through literary strategies. Reading him requires "against-the-grain" analysis, recognizing where partisan purpose shapes narrative while extracting valuable details he couldn't entirely fabricate.

## Accessing Liutprand: editions, translations, and scholarly resources

## Creating believable fiction from fragmentary evidence

For historical fiction writers, Liutprand offers rare advantages: a 10th-century figure whose personality leaps from the page, whose emotional reactions and prejudices are documented, whose voice—sardonic, bitter, witty, learned—survives in his own words. Yet challenges remain in distinguishing literary persona from actual person.

### Character foundations: the contradictions

**Build him from contradictions** that sources document rather than resolving them artificially:

- Admired Byzantine culture (949) then despised it (968)—both genuine responses to different contexts
- Christian bishop who constantly quoted pagan classics and wrote sexually explicit passages
- Claimed humility while displaying arrogant intellectual pride
- Called others corrupt while admitting own envy and greed
- Sophisticated rhetorician who deployed crude scatological humor
- Served God but primarily served power

**His emotional range is documented**: vengeful (titled his magnum opus "Revenge"), envious (admitted openly), witty (compared to Herodotus), proud (wounded pride drove diplomatic failures), clever with words, quick to take offense, never forgetting slights.

### Speech patterns and mannerisms

**Linguistic signatures** fiction writers can deploy:

- Sprinkling Greek phrases to show off: "ἀδελφοί μου, adelfi mi" before translating
- Classical quotations dropped into conversation, especially Virgil and Horace
- Sarcastic tone, biting wit, etymological wordplay
- Biblical allusions for moral authority
- Can shift from elevated philosophical discourse to earthy vulgarity
- Long complex sentences when serious; short barbs when attacking

**Physical evidence is limited** but suggestive:

- "Wonderful voice" that won favor—suggests good breath control, possibly robust
- Traveled extensively including arduous Constantinople journey—reasonable health
- Active diplomatic career—physical capability
- Lived to approximately 52 (died 972)—decent lifespan for era
- No physical disabilities mentioned

### Believable scenes and situations

**Documented incidents** provide fictional scaffolding:

1. **Age 11 (931)**: Singing at King Hugh's court, winning favor through vocal talent
2. **949 embassy**: Being carried on eunuchs' shoulders, witnessing mechanical throne rise, making impudent Lazarus/Dives joke to Constantine, receiving gold coins
3. **c. 950**: Bitter quarrel with Berengar II, property/status loss
4. **955**: Fleeing to Otto's court, starting over in foreign land
5. **956**: Meeting Recemund of Elvira at Frankfurt, being encouraged to write history
6. **958-962**: Writing *Antapodosis* by candlelight, interrupted by diplomatic missions
7. **963**: Participating in synod deposing Pope John XII
8. **968 Constantinople**: Waiting in rain outside walls, eating despised vegetables, buying purple textiles, confronting officials, walking out of banquet, falling ill from depression, scrawling bitter poem on walls

- Court attendance, synods, papal elections

- Collecting classical manuscripts, studying Greek

**His worldview** shaped by:

- Lombardist patriotism (wanted Lombard kingdom's growth and freedom)
- Class consciousness (aristocratic background)
- Cultural defensiveness (called "barbarian" by Byzantines)
- Pragmatic religious conviction (served God but opportunistically)
- Stoic resignation mixed with active vengeance philosophy
- Awareness of performing roles—"inner vs. outer man"

### The arc of transformation

The **narrative structure history provides** is extraordinary: young admirer of Byzantine culture (949) transforms into bitter enemy (968) through personal humiliation and political rivalry, then dies shortly after (972) without seeing final vindication. His literary revenge through *Relatio* succeeds where diplomacy failed—the text shapes Western perceptions of Byzantium for centuries.

**Fiction can explore the psychological journey**: How does a 29-year-old impressed by Constantinople's wonders become a 48-year-old who calls its emperor a "monstrosity"? Was it purely political expediency, or genuine transformation? How did service to three patrons (Hugh's court → Berengar → Otto) shape his identity? What did it cost him psychologically to constantly reinvent himself?

**The incomplete *Antapodosis*** suggests intriguing possibilities: Did literary revenge satisfy him enough that finishing seemed pointless? Did Otto's demands redirect his energy? Did he realize revenge achieved through writing surpassed revenge through politics?

### What we'll never know

Gaps in evidence offer fictional freedom while demanding respect for historical limits:

- **No physical description** from contemporaries—height, coloring, build unknown except "wonderful voice"
- **Specific cause** of Berengar quarrel unclear—hints at property/status loss but no details
- **Personal relationships**—any family? Close friendships? Romantic connections? (As cleric/bishop, celibate officially, but 10th-century clergy...)
- **Inner thoughts** on faith versus pragmatism—did he believe his propaganda?
- **Final years** (970-972)—what did he do? Continue writing? Reflect on failures?
- **Exact circumstances of death**—peacefully in Cremona? During travel?

Fiction can explore these gaps while remaining true to documented character: turbulent, envious, witty, learned, vindictive, sophisticated, crude, proud, clever—a man who understood written words could become tools of self-invention and literary revenge, who left behind personae rather than personality, whose voice across a millennium remains vivid, problematic, and utterly human.

## Conclusion: a witness who reveals more than he intends

Liutprand of Cremona endures as historical source and potential fictional protagonist because of, not despite, his flaws. His biases themselves reveal crucial information about 10th-century political culture, diplomatic practice, cultural boundaries, and how individuals navigated shifting allegiances in a fragmented European landscape. His transformation from Berengar's chancellor to Otto's propagandist, from Byzantine admirer to Byzantine critic, charts not just personal trajectory but broader historical currents: the assertion of Western imperial claims, growing East-West Christian divide, and emergence of cultural stereotypes that would shape medieval consciousness.

For fiction writers, he offers something precious: **a fully realized historical character whose personality, quirks, prejudices, emotional reactions, and voice survive in remarkable detail**. His works let us hear a 10th-century mind thinking—sophisticated yet crude, learned yet prejudiced, capable of both elevated rhetoric and scatological humor. The contrast between his 949 and 968 embassies provides ready-made character arc, while gaps in evidence offer creative freedom within historical bounds.

Modern scholarship's recognition that he created literary personae rather than transparent autobiography liberates fiction writers: you're not violating history by imagining the "real" Liutprand behind his self-dramatizations. That gap between performance and person is historically documented. **Your task becomes psychological speculation grounded in textual evidence**—exploring how someone who understood written self-invention so acutely might have experienced the discrepancy between public persona and private self, between diplomatic failure and literary triumph, between serving power and serving legacy.

His ultimate achievement was transforming humiliation into literature so vivid it shaped Western perceptions for centuries. That's the novelist's craft: transmuting experience into narrative that outlives its creator. Liutprand understood this implicitly, crafting revenge that succeeded where diplomacy failed. A thousand years later, we remember Nikephoros Phokas primarily through Liutprand's savage portrait. **The diplomat lost; the writer won.** That tension—between political failure and literary success, between the person who lived and the personae who survive—makes him endlessly fascinating for both historians and fiction writers seeking to resurrect the medieval world in all its complexity, prejudice, humor, and humanity.
