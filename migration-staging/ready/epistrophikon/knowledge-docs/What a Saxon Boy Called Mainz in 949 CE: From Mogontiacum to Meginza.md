# What a Saxon boy called Mainz in 949 CE

**Around 940–950 CE, the city we know as Mainz was called *Meginza* (or the slightly older *Maginza*) in the Old High German vernacular, and *Moguntia* in the Latin of Ottonian charters and chronicles.** A Saxon-born speaker would have pronounced it subtly but distinctly differently from his Franconian neighbors — rendering the medial *-g-* as a soft velar fricative [ɣ] rather than a hard stop [g]. This linguistic detail, corroborated by a near-contemporary Arabic source, offers a vivid marker of regional identity for historical fiction set in this period. The name had already traveled far from its Celtic-Roman origins as *Mogontiacum*, and still had centuries of evolution ahead before settling into modern *Mainz*.

## From a Celtic god to a Roman fortress

The name ultimately traces to **Proto-Celtic *mogont-s***, meaning "the Great One" or "the Mighty One," a deity identified on altar inscriptions across Roman Britain and Gaul as Mogon, Mogounus, or Mogons. The Romans who established a military camp at the site in **13/12 BCE** adopted the Celtic settlement name as *Mogontiacum* — built from three Celtic elements: the god's name, a suffix *-ontiu-*, and the affiliation marker *-āko*, Latinized as *-(i)acum*. The compound meant, roughly, "estate of Mogon."

**Mogontiacum** remained the standard Latin form for approximately 500 years of Roman rule. Tacitus used it (with a variant *Magontiacum*) in the early second century; Ptolemy rendered it in Greek as *Mokontiakon*; the Tabula Peutingeriana abbreviates it to *Moguntiaco*. A vowel shift in the second syllable — *o* to *u* — appeared early, producing the common variant **Moguntiacum**, which reflects natural spoken-Latin phonology rather than a distinct historical break.

## The medieval Latin metamorphosis

The critical structural change came in Late Antiquity and the Merovingian period (sixth–seventh centuries), when the *-(i)acum* suffix eroded entirely. By the time of Venantius Fortunatus in the **sixth century**, the simplified form **Moguntia** — a first-declension feminine noun — was already in use. Seventh-century sources attest further variants: *Mogancia*, *Magancia*, *Maguntia*. The Carolingian period standardized around **Magontia/Moguntia**.

By the **tenth century**, the classical form *Mogontiacum* was essentially extinct in living documents. Ottonian royal charters used **Moguntia** as the standard city name, declined normally: *Moguntiae* (locative, "at Mainz"), *Moguntiam* (accusative, "to Mainz"). The adjective **Moguntinus/a/um** dominated official and ecclesiastical usage. One notable exception: **Widukind of Corvey**, writing his *Res gestae Saxonicae* around 967–973, employed the slightly archaic adjectival form *Mogontiacae* in the phrase *"summi pontificatus Mogontiacae sedis fastigium"* — "the eminence of the supreme pontificate of the Mogontine see." This conservative usage coexisted with the standard *Moguntinus* found in charters and episcopal catalogs. Educated churchmen would have recognized *Mogontiacum* from reading classical texts, but would not have used it in contemporary writing.

## The vernacular names: *Maginza* and *Meginza*

The Old High German forms derived not from the full *Mogontiacum* but from the shortened medieval Latin *Magontia/Moguntia*. The phonological pathway was: Late Latin palatalization turned the *-tia* ending into an affricate /tsja/, yielding the characteristic *-nza* ending in Germanic. Two OHG forms coexisted in the ninth and tenth centuries:

**Maginza** /maɡintsa/ was the older, more conservative form, preserving the original *a* in the first syllable. **Meginza** /meɡintsa/ showed *i*-umlaut — the /i/ in the second syllable fronting the first-syllable /a/ to /e/ — and was increasingly dominant by **circa 940 CE**. Both are catalogued in the onomastic tradition descending from Ernst Förstemann's *Altdeutsches Namenbuch*. Independent corroboration comes from the **medieval Hebrew form *Magenza*** (מגנצא), used by the Jewish community of Mainz (the "M" in the famous ShUM cities), which preserves the older first-syllable vowel while showing the weakened second syllable.

The subsequent evolution was: *Meginza* → *Megenze* (late OHG/early MHG, with vowel weakening) → *Meinze* (Middle High German, the intervocalic *-g-* vocalizing and merging into a diphthong) → *Meintz/Mentz* (fourteenth–fifteenth centuries) → **Mainz** (stabilized by the sixteenth–seventeenth centuries). The modern Hessian dialect form *Meenz* or *Määnz* preserves yet another thread of this history.

## How a Saxon ear heard *Meginza* differently

No specifically Old Saxon written form for "Mainz" survives — unsurprisingly, since Mainz lay deep in Franconian territory and Old Saxon texts like the *Heliand* concern biblical narrative, not Rhenish geography. But **known phonological differences between Old Saxon and Old High German allow confident reconstruction** of how a Saxon speaker would have pronounced the name.

The single most telling difference involves the medial *-g-*. In Old High German (specifically the East Franconian dialect spoken at Fulda), the West Germanic voiced velar fricative */ɣ/ had already hardened to a full voiced velar **stop** [g] by the eighth century. A Franconian speaker said *Meginza* with a firm, clear [g]. In Old Saxon, however, **the fricative [ɣ] was preserved intervocalically** — it only surfaced as a stop after nasals or when geminated. Before front vowels like /i/, it could further palatalize toward [ʝ]. A Saxon speaker would thus have naturally rendered the name as approximately **[meɣintsa]** or **[meʝintsa]** — with a soft, throaty, or gliding sound where the Franconian had a hard *g*.

This reconstruction receives striking independent corroboration from **Ibrahim ibn Yaqub**, the Andalusi Jewish traveler who visited the court of Otto I around **965 CE** and recorded the city's name in Arabic as **مغانجة (*Maghānja*)**. The Arabic letter غ (*ghayn*) specifically represents the voiced velar fricative /ɣ/ — exactly the sound a Saxon or northern speaker would have produced. Whether Ibrahim's informants were themselves Saxon courtiers, or whether this reflects broader Rhenish dialectal variation, the Arabic rendering confirms that a fricative pronunciation of the medial consonant was audible in mid-tenth-century Mainz.

Other phonological differences between Old Saxon and OHG — notably the High German consonant shift affecting /p/ and /k/ — do not directly impact this particular name. The *-nz-* affricate [nts] derives from Latin palatalization, not from the Germanic consonant shift, so it would have been identical in both dialects. The vowels, too, were essentially the same. **The *-g-* pronunciation was the acoustic signature that would have marked someone as Saxon.**

## The Archbishop's Latin title and ecclesiastical language

For a cathedral canon serving at Mainz in 949, the ecclesiastical Latin terminology was well standardized. Archbishop **Frederick (Fridericus)**, who held the see from **937 to 954**, bore the title:

- **Fridericus archiepiscopus Moguntinus** — the standard form in charters and chronicles
- **archiepiscopus sanctae Moguntinae sedis** — the more formal style, "archbishop of the holy Moguntine see"

Related institutional terms used consistently across the MGH corpus include **ecclesia Moguntina** ("the Moguntine church," i.e., the archdiocese), **sedes Moguntina** ("the Moguntine see"), and the inhabitants as **Moguntini**. The variant spellings *Mogontinus* and *Maguntinus* also appear, reflecting the fluid *o/a* and *o/u* alternation that persisted throughout the medieval period. Widukind's use of the older adjectival form *Mogontiacae* shows that even within a single generation of writers, archaizing and contemporary forms coexisted — a learned monk might reach for the classical flavor, while a chancery scribe used the streamlined *Moguntinus*.

As Primate of Germany (**Primas Germaniae**), the Archbishop of Mainz was the senior ecclesiastical figure in the realm. The archchancellor role (*archicancellarius*) was formally attached to the see from the time of Archbishop Willigis (975), though the function existed earlier. A cathedral canon in 949 would have referred to his institution as the *ecclesia Moguntina* and his archbishop as the *archiepiscopus Moguntinus*.

## Practical recommendations for fiction set in 949

For a Saxon boy educated at Fulda whose uncle serves as a cathedral canon at Mainz, the linguistic texture should reflect several layers simultaneously:

**In Latin** — the language of instruction, liturgy, and formal discourse — the boy would say and write **Moguntia**, with the adjective **Moguntinus**. His teachers at Fulda might occasionally use the classicizing **Mogontiacae** (as Widukind did), but **Moguntia** was the workhorse form. The archbishop is *archiepiscopus Moguntinus*; the cathedral community is the *ecclesia Moguntina*.

**In vernacular speech**, the dominant form by 949 was almost certainly **Meginza**, with the older **Maginza** still recognizable but fading. The boy, immersed in the East Franconian dialect environment of Fulda, would have learned to say **[meɡintsa]** with a hard Franconian *g*. But in unguarded moments, or when speaking with fellow Saxons, his native pronunciation — **[meɣintsa]**, with the telltale soft fricative — might surface, marking him as a northerner just as surely as his handling of other consonants. This small phonological detail could serve as a naturalistic marker of identity and assimilation in the novel.

The key scholarly reference for this entire naming history is R. Heuser's study **"Mogontiacum, Moguntia, Meginze, Meintz, Menz, Mainz: Die Schreibung des Stadtnamens von der Antike bis zur Neuzeit"** (*Domblätter*, 2004), which traces the written forms century by century. For the Ottonian Latin forms, the critical editions in the **Monumenta Germaniae Historica** — particularly the *Diplomata* of Otto I and Widukind's *Res gestae Saxonicae* (ed. Hirsch & Lohmann, 1935) — provide the primary textual evidence.

## Conclusion

The naming history of Mainz is not a simple linear progression but a braided river of parallel forms — Latin, vernacular, dialectal, Hebrew, Arabic — flowing simultaneously through different communities and registers. In 949 CE, the city existed as **Moguntia** on parchment, **Meginza** on Franconian lips, and something softer — **[meɣintsa]** — in a Saxon throat. The Arabic *Maghānja* and Hebrew *Magenza* confirm that these were not merely theoretical reconstructions but living sounds heard by real travelers and residents. For a novelist, this layering offers rich material: the same city, called by subtly different names depending on who is speaking, in what language, and to whom — a linguistic microcosm of the polyglot, multi-ethnic Ottonian world.
