**Assistant** (2025-11-02 16:03:16):

 Great question! Actually, neither term quite fits for 10th century Byzantine Greek.

**Demotic Greek** (δημοτική) is a modern term for the vernacular Greek that emerged much later, really codified in the 19th-20th centuries in opposition to Katharevousa.

**Koine** (κοινή) refers to the common Greek of the Hellenistic and Roman periods (roughly 300 BCE - 300 CE), the language of the New Testament and early Church fathers.

What Domenico is speaking in 955 CE would be called **Medieval Greek** or **Middle Greek** (sometimes **Byzantine Greek** in modern scholarship, though that term has the same issues you're avoiding with "Byzantine Empire"). 

The more specific contemporary term would be **ἡ κοινὴ διάλεκτος** (hē koinē dialektos, "the common dialect") when referring to everyday speech, as opposed to:
- **ἡ ἀττικίζουσα** (hē attikizousa) - the "Atticizing" high literary style that imitated classical Attic Greek
- Or simply **ἡ Ῥωμαϊκὴ** (hē Rhōmaïkē) - "the Roman [language]" as they often called their own Greek

Domenico's brief reply would be **middle-register Greek** - educated and grammatically correct, but without the archaic/Atticizing flourishes that Theophylact's ceremonial opening would have. It's the difference between a Victorian proclamation and "I'm ready, sir" - both proper English, vastly different registers.

The beauty is that this tonal shift itself becomes part of the argument about Romanitas that will unfold!
