# Ethics as Information Architecture
## IAC26 Talk Outline — April 17, 2026 (11:30 AM - 12:00 PM)

**Duration**: 25 min talk + 5 min Q&A
**Frame**: Recognition — helping IA practitioners see their skills are essential for AI ethics

---

## SECTION 1: The Problem (3 min)

### Key Point
AI ethics is failing because it's treated as a policy problem, not an architecture problem.

### Slide Concepts
- **Slide 1**: Title slide (Ethics as Information Architecture)
- **Slide 2**: Visual metaphor — "guardrails bolted onto a cliff edge" or similar fragile-protection image
- **Slide 3**: Word: "AFTERTHOUGHT"

### Speaker Notes

Open with the failure mode everyone's seen:

"You've probably seen the headlines. AI systems doing things their creators swear they never intended. Jailbreaks. Content moderation failures. Systems that were supposed to help people instead causing harm.

The typical response is to bolt on protections after the fact. Warning messages. Terms of service. Content moderation that tries to catch bad outputs after they've already been generated.

This is ethics as afterthought. And it doesn't work.

When you treat ethics as interface-level features — something you add on top of the core capabilities — those protections are fragile. They can be circumvented by clever prompting. They can be undermined by business pressure. They're the guardrails bolted to the cliff edge, not the road that keeps you away from the cliff entirely."

**Transition**: "But you already know why this fails. You've known it for years."

---

## SECTION 2: The Insight (5 min)

### Key Point
Structure determines possibility. This is the core IA insight — and it's exactly what AI ethics needs.

### Slide Concepts
- **Slide 4**: Visual metaphor — information architecture shaping flow (maybe water finding its path through structure)
- **Slide 5**: Word: "STRUCTURE"
- **Slide 6**: Visual — AI systems as information spaces (inhabited spaces, hint at the social angle)

### Speaker Notes

This is where you meet them where they are:

"Information architects understand something crucial that most AI developers miss: structure determines possibility.

The way you organize information doesn't just affect usability. It defines what actions are even *possible* within a system. You've spent your careers shaping how people navigate information spaces. You know that architecture isn't neutral — it enables some behaviors and prevents others.

This is the insight AI ethics desperately needs.

When ethical principles are structural constraints on information flow — not policies, not guidelines, not terms of service — you create systems where certain outcomes simply can't occur. Not because they're prohibited, but because the architecture doesn't allow them.

[Pause]

There's something else you understand that matters here. Information spaces aren't just repositories. They're places where people do things, make decisions, interact. You've been designing *inhabited* spaces.

AI is creating new kinds of information spaces. And they have new kinds of inhabitants — not just humans anymore, but other forms of sapience participating in those spaces alongside us.

The ethical implications of that are heavy. And your skills are exactly what's needed to address them."

**Transition**: "Let me show you what this looks like in practice."

---

## SECTION 3: The Architecture (10 min)

### Key Point
How ethical principles become structural constraints — boundary enforcement, protection hierarchies, information flow.

### Slide Concepts
- **Slide 7**: Diagram — request flow with boundary enforcement BEFORE processing (simple: Input → Ethics Layer → Processing → Output)
- **Slide 8**: Word: "BEFORE" (emphasized — the key insight is pre-processing interception)
- **Slide 9**: Visual — protection hierarchy (like Asimov's laws as architecture)
- **Slide 10**: Visual — the four principles as structural constraints (Human Empowerment, System Integrity, Project Excellence, Professional Boundaries)
- **Slide 11**: Visual — human decision authority preserved (gateway/checkpoint metaphor)

### Speaker Notes

This is the meat of the talk — but keep it conceptual, not technical:

"I've been building an AI assistant called Piper Morgan with what I call ethics-first architecture. Let me show you how ethical principles become structure.

**First principle: Boundary enforcement happens BEFORE processing.**

[Point to diagram]

Every request passes through ethical boundary checking before any AI processing occurs. Not after — before. This isn't content moderation catching bad outputs. This is architecture that prevents certain inputs from ever reaching the AI in the first place.

Think of it like building a road that doesn't go near the cliff, rather than putting up guardrails at the edge.

**Second principle: Protection hierarchies resolve conflicts.**

You know how Asimov's laws of robotics work — first law overrides second, second overrides third. That's actually an architectural pattern.

When principles conflict — and they will — the system needs a hierarchy. Human agency trumps system capability. Privacy trumps learning efficiency. Safety trumps speed. These aren't judgment calls made in the moment. They're resolved architecturally, in advance.

**Third principle: Human decision authority is structurally preserved.**

There are certain decisions the AI simply cannot make. Not because it's told not to — because the architecture doesn't give it the capability. It can inform, recommend, prepare — but the decision gateway requires human action to pass through.

This is the opposite of the 'ask forgiveness later' pattern we see in a lot of AI systems. It's 'you literally cannot proceed without human approval' built into the structure.

**Fourth principle: Professional boundaries at the structural level.**

[Brief — this connects to the social space theme]

Piper is designed to be a colleague, not a tool and not a friend. That's not a personality setting. It's a structural constraint on what kinds of interactions are possible. The system can't be manipulated into inappropriate relationships because those interaction patterns don't exist in the architecture.

[Pause]

None of this is magic. It's information architecture. You're defining what information flows where, what actions are possible at each point, what hierarchies govern conflicts. You've been doing this for decades. The domain is new; the skills aren't."

**Transition**: "You might be wondering — does this actually work?"

## SECTION 4: The Proof (3 min)

### Key Point
Metadata learning proves privacy-preserving intelligence is achievable. Privacy as architectural choice.

### Slide Concepts
- **Slide 12**: Number: "80.3%" (the clustering accuracy figure)
- **Slide 13**: Visual metaphor — learning from relationships, not content (maybe constellation/network vs. reading documents)
- **Slide 14**: Word: "CHOICE"

### Speaker Notes

Keep this tight — it's evidence, not a deep dive:

"Here's empirical proof that architectural ethics works.

Piper learns from metadata relationships — not content. It understands how documents relate to each other, when they're accessed, what patterns of work they're part of. It never reads the documents themselves.

We achieved 80.3% clustering accuracy using only relationship metadata. Zero content analysis required.

[Let that land]

This matters because it proves something important: privacy-preserving AI isn't just theoretically nice. It's practically achievable. You don't have to read everyone's documents to provide intelligent assistance.

We didn't do this because we had to. We did it because privacy is a design constraint, not a feature request. It's an architectural choice — and the architecture still works.

The same is true for every ethical constraint. When you build them into the structure, you don't lose capability. You gain trust."

**Transition**: "Which brings me to why I'm really here."

---

## SECTION 5: The Call (4 min)

### Key Point
IA practitioners are essential for responsible AI development. You're not being made obsolete — you're desperately needed.

### Slide Concepts
- **Slide 15**: Visual — information spaces with multiple kinds of inhabitants (humans + AI agents)
- **Slide 16**: Word: "NEEDED"
- **Slide 17**: Closing slide (title + contact)

### Speaker Notes

This is the emotional peak — the recognition moment:

"I said earlier that AI is creating new kinds of information spaces with new kinds of inhabitants.

Let me be direct about what that means.

We're building systems where non-human but sapient actors participate in social interfaces alongside humans. They use language. They make decisions. They form something like relationships. They inhabit the spaces we design.

[Pause]

Information architects have spent decades understanding how structure shapes behavior in information spaces. How architecture enables some outcomes and prevents others. How to design spaces that serve human flourishing.

That expertise isn't being made obsolete by AI. It's desperately needed.

The people building AI systems right now are mostly engineers and data scientists. They're brilliant at capabilities. They're often naive about the spaces those capabilities create — the social implications, the ethical weight of designed environments, what it means to build places where people *live* and *work* and now *share space with artificial minds*.

You understand that. You've been thinking about it your whole careers.

[Pause — this is the key line]

So my ask is simple: don't cede this territory. Don't assume AI development is someone else's problem. The complexity of AI doesn't require abandoning human-centered design — it requires information architects who can structure these systems so that harmful patterns become impossible, privacy is preserved through architectural choice, and human agency remains supreme.

Not through policy. Through structure.

You already know how to do this. The domain is new. The stakes are higher. But the skills are yours.

Use them."

**End**
