# Ethics as Information Architecture — Speaker Notes

## Slide 1: Title (ETHICS as INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE)

Open with presence. Let the title land. "I'm Christian Crumlish, and I've been building an AI assistant called Piper Morgan. Today I want to talk about something I think you already understand better than most people in this room realize."

---

## Slide 2: AFTERTHOUGHT

"You've seen the headlines. AI systems doing things their creators swear they never intended. Jailbreaks. Content moderation failures. The typical response is to bolt on protections after the fact. Warning messages. Terms of service. Content moderation catching bad outputs after they've already been generated. This is ethics as afterthought. And it doesn't work."

---

## Slide 3: Guardrails at the cliff edge vs. a road that doesn't go there

"When you treat ethics as interface-level features — something you add on top — those protections are fragile. They can be circumvented. They can be undermined by business pressure. They're the guardrails bolted to the cliff edge, not the road that keeps you away from the cliff entirely." [Pause] "But you already know why this fails. You've known it for years."

---

## Slide 4: STRUCTURE

"Information architects understand something crucial that most AI developers miss: structure determines possibility. The way you organize information doesn't just affect usability. It defines what actions are even *possible* within a system. You've spent your careers shaping how people navigate information spaces. You know that architecture isn't neutral — it enables some behaviors and prevents others."

---

## Slide 5: You've known this for years.

Let this land. Brief pause. "This is the insight AI ethics desperately needs. When ethical principles are structural constraints on information flow — not policies, not guidelines, not terms of service — you create systems where certain outcomes simply can't occur. Not because they're prohibited, but because the architecture doesn't allow them."

---

## Slide 6: INHABITED

"There's something else you understand that matters here. Information spaces aren't just repositories. They're places where people do things, make decisions, interact. You've been designing *inhabited* spaces. AI is creating new kinds of information spaces with new kinds of inhabitants — not just humans anymore, but other forms of sapience participating alongside us."

---

## Slide 7: BEFORE

"Let me show you what this looks like in practice. First principle: boundary enforcement happens BEFORE processing. Every request passes through ethical boundary checking before any AI processing occurs. Not after — before. This isn't content moderation catching bad outputs. This is architecture that prevents certain inputs from ever reaching the AI in the first place."

---

## Slide 8: Flow diagram (Input → Ethics Layer → Processing)

Point to the diagram. "Think of it like building a road that doesn't go near the cliff, rather than putting up guardrails at the edge. The ethics layer intercepts requests before the AI ever sees them. Structural prevention, not reactive filtering."

---

## Slide 9: HIERARCHY

"Second principle: protection hierarchies resolve conflicts. You know how Asimov's laws of robotics work — first law overrides second, second overrides third. That's actually an architectural pattern. When principles conflict — and they will — the system needs a hierarchy. Human agency trumps system capability. Privacy trumps learning efficiency. These aren't judgment calls made in the moment. They're resolved architecturally, in advance."

---

## Slide 10: Some decisions require human action to proceed

"Third principle: human decision authority is structurally preserved. There are certain decisions the AI simply cannot make. Not because it's told not to — because the architecture doesn't give it the capability. It can inform, recommend, prepare — but the decision gateway requires human action to pass through. This is the opposite of 'ask forgiveness later.' It's 'you literally cannot proceed without human approval' built into the structure."

---

## Slide 11: 80.3%

Let the number land. "Here's empirical proof that architectural ethics works. Piper learns from metadata relationships — not content. It understands how documents relate to each other, when they're accessed, what patterns of work they're part of. It never reads the documents themselves. We achieved 80.3% clustering accuracy using only relationship metadata. Zero content analysis required." [Pause] "Privacy-preserving AI isn't just theoretically nice. It's practically achievable."

---

## Slide 12: CHOICE

"We didn't do this because we had to. We did it because privacy is a design constraint, not a feature request. It's an architectural choice — and the architecture still works. When you build ethical constraints into the structure, you don't lose capability. You gain trust."

---

## Slide 13: NEEDED

"Which brings me to why I'm really here. We're building systems where non-human but sapient actors participate in social interfaces alongside humans. They use language. They make decisions. They form something like relationships. They inhabit the spaces we design." [Pause] "Information architects have spent decades understanding how structure shapes behavior in information spaces. That expertise isn't being made obsolete by AI. It's desperately needed."

---

## Slide 14: Don't cede this territory.

"The people building AI systems right now are mostly engineers and data scientists. They're brilliant at capabilities. They're often naive about the spaces those capabilities create — the social implications, the ethical weight of designed environments, what it means to build places where people live and work and now share space with artificial minds. You understand that. You've been thinking about it your whole careers."

---

## Slide 15: Not through policy. Through structure.

This is the emotional peak. Slow down. "So my ask is simple: don't cede this territory. Don't assume AI development is someone else's problem. The complexity of AI doesn't require abandoning human-centered design — it requires information architects who can structure these systems so that harmful patterns become impossible, privacy is preserved through architectural choice, and human agency remains supreme. Not through policy. Through structure. You already know how to do this."

---

## Slide 16: Contact slide

"The domain is new. The stakes are higher. But the skills are yours. Use them." [Pause for applause/transition to Q&A] "I'm happy to take questions."
