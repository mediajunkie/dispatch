## The Trust Gradient as Innovation Framework

Today's PDR work formalized a "trust gradient" for how Piper earns the right to be proactive:

| Stage | Behavior | How Earned |
|-------|----------|------------|
| 1 (New) | Responds only | Default |
| 2 (Building) | Offers related capabilities | ~10 successful interactions |
| 3 (Established) | Proactive from context | ~50 interactions + patterns |
| 4 (Trusted) | Anticipates needs | Extended history |

**Innovation angle**: This isn't just UXâ€”it's a model for human-AI collaboration generally. Most AI products are stuck at Stage 1 (respond only) or jump straight to Stage 4 (autonomous agents) without earning trust. The gradient acknowledges that trust is built incrementally through demonstrated competence.

**Question**: Is the trust gradient pattern applicable beyond Piper? Could it be a framework we publish/teach?

## The "10%/90%" Discovery Problem

Research shows users discover ~10% of capabilities during onboarding and ~90% through ongoing use. Most products optimize for the 10% (feature tours, tooltips, tutorials).

Our PDRs flip this: FTUX teaches users *how to discover*, not *what exists*. The onboarding is successful if users can find features they weren't explicitly taught.

**Innovation angle**: "Discovery architecture" might be an underexplored category. Not information architecture (where things are) or interaction design (how things work), but discovery design (how users learn what's possible).

**Question**: Is discovery architecture a distinct discipline? Could Piper become known for excellence in this area?
