# When Three PM Thought Leaders Built the Same Framework

**Three of product management's most influential coaches—Christina Wodtke, Kate Leto, and Petra Wille—independently developed remarkably similar "role canvas" frameworks within a few years of each other, revealing a deep truth about the PM profession's most pressing problem: role ambiguity.** This convergence wasn't coincidence or copying; it was the PM community simultaneously discovering that the field's greatest challenge isn't shipping features—it's defining what product managers are actually supposed to do.

## The parallel invention that revealed PM's hidden crisis

Between 2016 and 2020, each thought leader faced the same client problem: organizations struggling to define, hire for, and develop product managers. Their independent solutions converged on nearly identical visual frameworks that map the dimensions of a PM role. This "multiple discovery" phenomenon—where independent practitioners arrive at similar solutions from shared problems—echoes historical parallels like Newton and Leibniz independently developing calculus, or Bell and Gray filing telephone patents on the same day.

Christina Wodtke's mention of **cryptomnesia** is telling. Cryptomnesia is unconscious plagiarism—when a forgotten memory returns as what feels like an original idea. The term dates to the 1870s and has famously affected Nietzsche (who reproduced passages from childhood books in *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*) and George Harrison (whose "My Sweet Lord" contained substantial portions from "He's So Fine"). The line between cryptomnesia and true zeitgeist-driven parallel invention is notoriously hazy. For these three PM leaders—who attend the same conferences, read similar literature, and face identical client problems—the distinction may be moot. What matters is that **three different minds, solving the same problem, landed on the same structural insight**: PM roles need visual frameworks that decompose responsibility into distinct, assessable dimensions.

## Christina Wodtke's Role Profile: goals, responsibilities, knowledge, skills

Christina Wodtke developed her **Role Profile Canvas** as part of her 2019 book *The Team That Managed Itself*, building on earlier blog work like "Let the Right One In." Her framework positions hiring as the first step of a **Role Lifecycle** with three phases: Set the Role, Check Performance and Support Growth, and Evaluate and Correct.

The Role Profile contains four components:

**Goals** represent objectives aligned to company OKRs—time-bound achievements like "build an internal design team" or "create a new product category." **Responsibilities** capture steady-state activities inherent to the role: managing budgets, coaching direct reports, keeping upper management informed. **Market Knowledge** assesses domain expertise—how well someone understands e-commerce, healthcare, or whatever space the company operates in. **Skills** encompass both hard and soft capabilities needed to accomplish the job.

Wodtke recommends freelisting skills, stack-ranking them, then drawing a line between must-have and nice-to-have. Before interviews, she advocates writing questions for each quadrant that elicit experience-based stories rather than speculation. "Are you good at dealing with conflict?" gets wishful answers; "Tell me about a time when your team experienced conflict" gets truth.

Critically, she treats this as a **living document**—reviewed before one-on-ones, updated as the role evolves, used for quarterly performance conversations. The same framework that screens candidates becomes the instrument for ongoing development and evaluation.

## Petra Wille's PMwheel: eight dimensions of PM competence

Petra Wille created her **PMwheel** in 2016 while working with a tech company that asked her to help product managers "understand the role better." She needed a framework for assessment and coaching conversations—so she built one. The PMwheel became public around 2020 and features in her book *Strong Product People* (2021).

The wheel measures **eight key activities** scored on a 0-7 scale:

- **Understand the Problem**: Awareness of user problems, motives, and company needs
- **Find a Solution**: Identifying good problems, generating solutions, designing experiments
- **Do Some Planning**: Roadmapping or agile planning, ability to tell the "what's next" story
- **Get it Done**: Working with development teams to ship, stakeholder communication, quality ownership
- **Listen & Learn**: Observing usage, iterating on learnings, knowing performance metrics at any time
- **Team**: Lateral leadership, motivation, teamwork capability
- **Grow!**: Investment in personal development, staying current on tech/UX/business trends
- **Agile**: Deep understanding of agile values and principles, not just surface-level practice

Assessments create a **spiderweb visualization** that immediately surfaces development priorities. Wille pairs this with a **Future-Self Canvas**—a commitment document with As-Is state, To-Be vision, specific actions, and timeframe.

She also developed a separate **Role Definition Canvas** for organizations that lack written PM role definitions. Her philosophy: "It's not about filling the blanks. It's about the process, the discussions you will have to come up with an agreed-on version." Role clarity must come first—before the PMwheel can be meaningfully applied.

## Kate Leto's Role Canvas: purpose, accountability, human and technical skills

Kate Leto's **Role Canvas** appears in her 2020 book *Hiring Product Managers: Using Product EQ to Go Beyond Culture and Skills*. She developed it while helping organizations shift from tactical job descriptions to meaningful role definitions.

Her canvas poses four deceptively simple questions:

**What is the purpose of the role?** Not restating the job title, but articulating why the role exists—for example, "to lead a team to find new ways to engage and increase profitability with a new millennial market." **What is the role accountable for?** The outcomes it owns: launching specific features, hitting customer service ratings, building cross-functional teams. **What human skills will the role need?** Context-specific capabilities like conflict resolution, influence, empathy, or resilience—prioritized based on team needs. **What technical skills will the role need?** PM-specific capabilities: roadmapping, A/B testing, pricing, OKRs, Jobs-to-be-Done methodology.

Leto's distinctive contribution is the **Product EQ** framework—emphasizing that human skills and technical skills must be balanced. She developed a **Product EQ Wheel** for assessing soft skills and a **Product Practice Canvas** for self-reflection on that balance. Her core insight: "If you're going to hire product managers with Product EQ, you need to have it yourself. If you don't have it, you can't see it."

She recommends running Role Canvas workshops with 10 or fewer participants—hiring managers, team members, stakeholders, recruiters—using sticky notes and dot-voting to reach alignment. The canvas remains dynamic, updated as the team meets candidates and learns more.

## Why the convergence happened: shared problems, shared solutions

The three frameworks emerged from identical client pain points. All three consultants repeatedly encountered organizations with unclear PM expectations, generic job descriptions, inconsistent hiring, and no development pathways. The PM role's notorious ambiguity—sitting at the intersection of business, technology, and user experience—makes it uniquely susceptible to role confusion.

Several factors enabled parallel invention:

**Shared foundational exposure**: All three draw from similar intellectual traditions—coaching frameworks, the Business Model Canvas format, agile methodology, Marty Cagan's work. Petra Wille explicitly learned from Cagan during her time at XING. Kate Leto's Product EQ builds on Daniel Goleman's emotional intelligence research. Christina Wodtke connects to OKR methodology.

**The canvas format was "in the air"**: By 2016, visual canvases had become standard tools in business design. The Lean Canvas, Value Proposition Canvas, and Business Model Canvas established the template—a single page with distinct boxes forcing clarity and enabling conversation.

**Same professional network**: All three participate in the same ecosystem. They've appeared together at Product @ Heart Conference in Hamburg for book signings alongside Martin Eriksson, Bruce McCarthy, and Rich Mironov. They read each other's work, recommend each other's books, and face the same client problems.

The convergence validates the insight: **when multiple experts independently discover the same structural solution, that solution probably maps to something real about the problem space**.

## Application to AI PM assistants: role clarity for human-AI collaboration

The PM Role Canvas concept carries profound implications for AI systems designed to assist product managers. An AI PM assistant faces a version of the same problem these canvases solve: **understanding what it's supposed to do, and what humans expect of it**.

Consider how each canvas dimension translates to AI context:

| Human PM Canvas Dimension | AI Assistant Equivalent |
|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Purpose | Why does this AI exist? What human capability does it augment? |
| Responsibilities | What steady-state tasks should the AI handle vs. escalate? |
| Accountability | What outcomes is the AI responsible for vs. advisory on? |
| Human Skills | How should the AI communicate, collaborate, and handle uncertainty? |
| Technical Skills | What PM capabilities (research synthesis, roadmap analysis, prioritization) should it perform? |
| Market Knowledge | What domain context must it understand to be useful? |
| Goals | What specific objectives is it helping achieve? |
| Grow! | How should it learn and improve from interactions? |

An AI PM assistant could use a Role Canvas-like framework to:

- **Establish boundaries** between tasks it should autonomously complete, tasks requiring human approval, and tasks it should only support
- **Clarify handoff points** in human-AI collaborative workflows
- **Surface role ambiguity** when instructions conflict with its defined purpose
- **Adapt behavior** based on the human PM's own role definition—supporting different activities for a strategic PM versus an execution-focused PM
- **Communicate limitations** transparently when asked to operate outside its role

The "human skills" dimension is particularly relevant. Just as Kate Leto emphasizes that human skills aren't optional extras but prerequisites for technical effectiveness, an AI assistant's communication style, uncertainty handling, and collaborative approach fundamentally shape its utility.

## Conclusion: role clarity as the foundation of effective PM practice

The simultaneous invention of PM role canvases by Wodtke, Leto, and Wille reveals that role ambiguity isn't a minor operational problem—it's the foundation on which all PM dysfunction rests. Bad hires, ineffective performance conversations, and stalled career development all trace back to the same root cause: nobody agreed on what the job actually is.

Each framework attacks this from a slightly different angle: Wodtke emphasizes the role lifecycle and ongoing evolution; Wille focuses on assessment and development planning; Leto highlights the balance between human and technical skills. Together, they form a comprehensive toolkit for one of PM's most neglected challenges.

For AI systems entering the PM workflow, these frameworks offer more than historical curiosity. They provide a structural language for defining what an AI assistant should and shouldn't do—a kind of **machine role canvas** that enables clearer human-AI collaboration. The same insight that helped three thought leaders independently solve the PM role problem may prove essential for the next generation of AI-augmented product development.
