# UX Patterns and Design Challenges for LLM/AI Interfaces

**The emergence of large language models has created an unprecedented rupture in interface design orthodoxy.** For four decades, graphical user interfaces operated on principles of determinism, predictability, and explicit user control. LLMs violate each of these assumptions. The same prompt yields different outputs. Users struggle to articulate needs in natural language. AI systems can now act autonomously in the world. This synthesis examines eight fundamental design challenges that practitioners face when building AI interfaces, drawing on academic research, shipped product implementations, and emerging pattern libraries to map both convergences and productive tensions in this rapidly evolving field.

The stakes are substantial. According to Greg Nudelman, **85% of AI initiatives fail**, with UX failures representing a significant contributor. Yet the design community has no established playbookâ€”practitioners are inventing patterns in real-time, often discovering that solutions to one challenge create problems elsewhere. Trust mechanisms that help users calibrate confidence may increase cognitive load. Memory systems that enable continuity raise privacy concerns. Agent interfaces that provide observability may overwhelm users with information. The eight thematic clusters examined here represent not just discrete challenges but an interconnected system of design tradeoffs that must be navigated together.

## 1. Trust and calibration remain the foundational challenge

The question of how users calibrate appropriate trust in AI outputs represents the most thoroughly researched yet persistently unsolved challenge in AI interface design. Two landmark academic contributions provide the theoretical foundation. Amershi et al.'s 2019 CHI paper established **18 validated guidelines for human-AI interaction**, with trust-relevant principles including making clear what the system can do (G1), making clear how well it can do it (G2), and enabling efficient correction when AI errs (G9). Zhang, Liao, and Bellamy's 2020 FAccT research delivered a more sobering finding: confidence scores can help calibrate trust, but **trust calibration alone is not sufficient to improve decision-making outcomes**. Even when users develop appropriate skepticism, they need sufficient domain knowledge to complement AI's errors.

The practical design space for displaying confidence spans four distinct approaches, each with significant tradeoffs. Numeric approaches like token-level logprobs offer precision and enable computational thresholds for fallback behavior, but research indicates LLM confidence scores are often poorly calibratedâ€”better-performing models show more aligned overall confidence, yet even accurate models show **minimal variation between right and wrong answers**. Categorical approaches using high/medium/low indicators simplify interpretation but sacrifice nuance. IBM Carbon for AI uses visual metaphors including brightness and glow to "illuminate" AI-generated content, while linguistic hedging through epistemic markers ("I think," "This might be") matches how humans naturally express uncertainty. Research by Tang et al. found that self-verbalized uncertainty estimates are more easily interpretable, though LLMs often exhibit high overconfidence when verbalizing confidenceâ€”training protocols fine-tune outputs toward human preferences, which often bias against uncertainty expressions.

The tension between algorithm aversion and automation complacency frames the calibration challenge as a spectrum problem rather than a binary. Automation bias leads users to over-rely on AI recommendations, forming what Cummings describes as a "perfection scheme" where monitoring attention relaxes as automation increases. Algorithm aversion, documented by Dietvorst et al., creates under-reliance even when AI demonstrably outperforms human judgment. Jones-Jang and Park's 2023 research reveals the paradox: **initial automation bias leads to AI error observation, which triggers acute disappointment, which produces algorithmic aversion**. Both represent miscalibrated trust lying at opposite ends of a spectrum where "algorithmic vigilance" represents the unrealized ideal midpoint.

Shipped products have taken notably different approaches to the transparency dimension of trust. Claude's extended thinking mode displays an expandable section showing problem-solving approach and processing duration, though Anthropic acknowledges significant tradeoffs: the visible thought process is "more detached and less personal-sounding," and critically, **"we don't know for certain that what's in the thought process truly represents what's going on in the model's mind."** Models often make decisions based on factors they don't explicitly discuss in their reasoning. ChatGPT's consumer interface relies primarily on linguistic hedging without explicit confidence displays, while the API provides token-level probability scores that developers can visualize. The Google PAIR Guidebook advocates for "explaining for understanding, not completeness" through progressive disclosure and model confidence displays.

Pattern libraries have achieved convergence on several principles while leaving others contested. Universal agreement exists that transparency is essential, that explanations should be user-centric rather than technically complete, that users need consistent visual indicators of AI presence, and that support for efficient correction when AI errs is mandatory. The contradictions cluster around whether numeric or linguistic confidence is preferable, how much reasoning to show, and how to respond to algorithm aversionâ€”some research suggests showing AI making small errors intentionally creates realistic expectations, while other work warns that any perceived error can trigger aversion.

Significant gaps remain in long-term trust dynamics across repeated interactions, domain-specific calibration approaches, multi-turn conversation uncertainty display, and cultural differences in trust calibration. The measurement challenge is itself a gap: difficulty measuring whether users have "appropriately calibrated" trust makes UX validation challenging. The fundamental open question is whether, given that LLM confidence shows minimal correlation with accuracy, confidence displays should be shown at all.

## 2. The conversation-artifact tension reshapes interface paradigms

The emergence of artifact-based interfaces like Claude Artifacts and OpenAI Canvas represents a fundamental acknowledgment that **chat is optimized for exploration and dialogue (process), while artifacts are optimized for deliverables and reuse (product)**. This tension manifests across multiple dimensions: conversations flow linearly while artifacts occupy space, chat messages are contextual and ephemeral while artifacts are designed to stand alone, and good conversation relies on back-references while good artifacts must be complete without conversation context.

Anthropic's Claude Artifacts, launched June 2024 with over **500 million artifacts created**, auto-generates separate panels for content that is substantial (typically over 15 lines), self-contained, likely to be edited or iterated, and complex enough to stand alone without conversation context. The technical implementation uses React/Next.js/Tailwind in sandboxed execution, maintaining a separate processing thread for asynchronous content generation. A version selector enables switching between iterations, while MCP integration and persistent storage extend artifacts beyond session boundaries. OpenAI Canvas, launched October 2024, took a different approachâ€”GPT-4o was specifically trained to make trigger decisions (83% correct rate for writing, 94% for coding) and determine edit granularity. The Canvas emphasis is on collaborative editing through a copy-editor metaphor, with selective highlighting for targeted modifications and "show changes" highlighting for version comparison.

The research foundation from Ink & Switch offers an alternative paradigm entirely. Their work on Potluck and Embark explores **gradual enrichment** where documents evolve into interactive software through live searches extracting structured data from freeform text. Rather than separating chat from artifacts, they investigate "notes â†’ apps" progressions. Geoffrey Litt notes the natural extension to LLMs: "AI could help with extracting structured data from messy raw text data, but still leave the user in control of deciding what kinds of computations to run."

Google's Generative UI announcement in November 2025 represents the most radical extensionâ€”generating entire user experiences rather than contained artifacts. Their approach creates web pages, games, tools, and applications dynamically, tailoring interface complexity to user context. The system uses Gemini 3's agentic coding capabilities to design and code custom interfaces on the fly. Limitations remain substantial: generation can take a minute or more, and occasional inaccuracies occur. Yet the conceptual implication is significantâ€”if optimal interface format itself should be generated, traditional artifact boundaries dissolve.

Convergences across implementations reveal consistent principles. All major platforms separate artifacts or canvases from conversation, recognizing that substantial content needs dedicated space. Version control has become universal, with every shipped product including history mechanisms. Iterative refinement through conversationâ€”generating artifacts then using chat to modify themâ€”preserves natural language interaction value while solving the "substantial output" problem. Both Claude and Canvas use threshold-based triggering with criteria-based decisions for when to auto-generate.

Contradictions cluster around triggering philosophy and artifact scope. Claude auto-generates when criteria are met; Canvas lets models decide with manual trigger fallback; Google Dynamic View currently requires explicit selection. OpenAI explicitly biased coding triggers against over-triggering "to avoid disrupting our power users." On scope, Claude and Canvas define predefined artifact types while Google Generative UI generates any interfaceâ€”constrained artifacts are more reliable but less flexible. The role of structure itself is contested: Claude emphasizes standalone well-structured outputs while Ink & Switch advocates starting with messy text and gradually adding structure.

Under-explored areas include multi-artifact orchestration (coordinating multiple related artifacts like a multi-file codebase), artifact-to-artifact communication within sessions, collaborative multi-user editing of AI-generated artifacts, and cross-platform artifact portability (no interoperability standards exist). The open questions concern triggering heuristics (when is "substantial" substantial enough?), the merge problem for branched conversation paths, context preservation as artifacts outlive originating conversations, and the platform question of whether artifacts should be an OS-level or application-level feature.

## 3. Agency and control ambiguity pervades AI interaction

The fundamental tension in agency design emerges from AI systems that are increasingly capable of autonomous action while users experience discomfort when they lose control. Research by Adam et al. demonstrates that **IS-invoked (system-initiated) delegation increases users' perceived self-threat** and decreases willingness to accept delegationâ€”a finding with profound implications for when AI should offer to help versus wait to be asked. The effect amplifies when users perceive less control after potential delegation.

Victor Dibia, Microsoft Research Principal Engineer and core AutoGen developer, articulated four foundational UX design principles for autonomous multi-agent systems in his 2025 AI.Engineer keynote. **Capability discovery** addresses how users understand what agents can do, advocating for capability presets, sample tasks, and complexity indicators that nudge users toward high-reliability examples. **Observability and provenance** ensures users can trace agent actions given that autonomous agents explore non-deterministic trajectories unknown until runtimeâ€”streaming all updates including structured plans, current steps, duration, and token costs builds trust and helps users learn limitations. **Interruptibility** allows pausing, resuming, or canceling actions, critical for multi-agent systems that may run for extended periods with real-world resource implications. **Cost-aware delegation** quantifies and communicates action costs, implementing risk classifiers that return low/medium/high levels to enable automatic low-risk execution while requiring explicit approval for higher-risk operations.

The Knight First Amendment Institute's five-level autonomy framework provides a complementary lens, defining user roles along a spectrum: Operator (direct control), Collaborator (shared work), Consultant (agent asks guidance when uncertain), Approver (agent proposes, user approves), and Observer (full agent autonomy with monitoring). The key insight is that autonomy can be treated as a deliberate design decision, **separate from capability and operational environment**â€”capable agents can still act with low autonomy by design choice.

Human-AI handoff patterns have matured through customer service implementations. Effective practices include warm handoff that swaps avatars without "rebooting" conversation, context preservation passing full transcripts plus AI-generated summaries to human agents, user-initiated versus system-initiated triggers, and queue transparency showing position and wait time. Intelligent routing based on sentiment and topic, visual clarity about who's handling interactions, and immediate full context transfer define the standard across Zendesk, Intercom, and Salesforce implementations.

Shipped products demonstrate varied implementations. Coding assistants like VS Code Cursor and WindSurf Agent Mode stream actions as agents make progress, showing current steps and plan structure. PatternFly ChatBot Extension implements AI labels on all AI-generated messages, deep thinking and tool calls components for observability, and source attribution with title, body, and link. The Microsoft HAX Toolkit's 18 guidelines organize interventions by interaction phase: making capabilities clear initially, providing contextually relevant information during interaction, supporting graceful failure when wrong, and allowing user control over time.

Convergences show near-universal agreement that transparency is non-negotiableâ€”users must always know when AI is acting and what it's doing. User control is paramount with interruptibility appearing across all frameworks. Graduated autonomy models with multiple spectrum points rather than binary automated/manual distinctions have become standard. Context preservation in transitions and risk-based gating for irreversible actions represent additional consensus points.

Contradictions reveal unresolved tensions. Transparency conflicts with cognitive loadâ€”full observability can overwhelm users, yet too little information destroys trust. Adam et al.'s finding that system-invoked delegation creates self-threat contradicts recommendations for proactive AI suggestions. The relationship between capability and autonomy remains contestedâ€”some frameworks link them while others explicitly separate the design choices. Research showing that AI literacy improves delegation decisions but lowers future AI usage intention creates a design paradox.

Gaps include mode-switching patterns within interactions (users shifting from directing to collaborating to receiving), multi-agent system UX where multiple agents collaborate, long-running AI tasks spanning days or weeks, persistent delegation patterns for "always-on" agents, and cross-platform agency consistency. The open questions address control granularity (per-action versus goal-level delegation), optimal default autonomy levels, "agent creep" as capabilities expand faster than user expectations, and cultural differences in autonomy preferences.

## 4. Memory creates expectation gaps between users and systems

User mental models of AI memory fundamentally misalign with technical reality. Research from ACM CHI 2025 on "Users' Expectations and Practices with Agent Memory" found that users have incomplete mental models of how agents remember and recall, conceptualize memory along hierarchies from general to specific knowledge, and desire memories to be "cleanly organized by categories." The gap between expectations of human-like relationship continuity and the technical reality of stateless models with injected context remains largely unbridged.

ChatGPT's memory implementation uses a dual-layer architecture: saved memories that users direct explicitly ("Remember I'm vegetarian") and chat history reference that automatically extracts insights from past conversations. The system stores memory in system prompt context via a "bio" tool and creates "Notable Past Conversation Topics Highlights" to profile users with confidence ratings. However, **February 2025 brought catastrophic memory failures**â€”a backend update caused users to lose years of accumulated context, with reports of 83% memory failure rates and over 300 active complaint threads. Users described memory that "saves random-ass things with no discernment." Memory remains unavailable in EU/UK/Switzerland due to GDPR compliance challenges.

Claude's approach emphasizes transparency through file-based markdown using CLAUDE.md files. A single editable Memory Summary document captures all memories viewable in settings, with project-scoped isolation preventing context bleed across workspaces. Hierarchical memory locations (Enterprise â†’ User â†’ Project â†’ Session) provide structure. Notably, Claude does **not use vector databases or semantic search**â€”prioritizing transparency over RAG complexity by loading entire curated documents into context. Anthropic trained Claude "not to proactively remember sensitive information (like health details) unless explicitly asked."

The Letta/MemGPT research architecture from UC Berkeley provides the foundational "LLM Operating System" for memory. Its two-tier hierarchy includes Core Memory (in-context, directly editable) for agent persona and user information, and External Memory (out-of-context) comprising archival memory via vector database and recall memory for conversation history. The key innovation is **self-editing memory**â€”agents actively manage their own memory via tools (core_memory_append, core_memory_replace, archival_memory_search). Recursive conversation summaries when context fills up, "heartbeat" mechanisms for multi-step tool calling, and "sleep-time agents" for background memory management extend the paradigm.

Technical approaches span a spectrum from vector databases enabling semantic search over large unstructured datasets, to memory blocks with in-context editable sections and character limits, to profile building through automatic background profiling, to file-based transparent markdown. Hybrid architectures with three layersâ€”in-context for recent turns, short-term for key facts with decay, and long-term for vector embeddingsâ€”represent emerging recommended practice.

The Shape of AI pattern library recommends marking the moment of capture with lightweight prompts ("Saved to memory"), supporting temporary or forgetful modes, allowing code switching between different memory collections (work versus personal), and providing "clear the cache" reset options without requiring account deletion. The global versus scoped memory questionâ€”whether to allow cross-context persistence or enforce workspace isolationâ€”remains contested.

Memory decay and forgetting represents a critical gap. Most systems only add memory, rarely removing unless user requests. Research directions include "intelligent decay" scoring memories by recency, relevance, and usefulness, drawing inspiration from Ebbinghaus forgetting curves. Memoripy library implements "Memory Decay and Reinforcement" that automatically forgets less relevant data. Cross-context identityâ€”AI that knows users across applicationsâ€”faces security and privacy challenges with no major products offering true cross-application AI identity. Memory bloat degrades quality as CLAUDE.md or ChatGPT memories grow, with models struggling to find relevant information in large context, yet no clear UX patterns help users prune or organize accumulated memory.

Open questions include handling contradictory memories, the right balance between automatic and explicit memory, designing "forgetting" UX where users understand decay without feeling loss, memory portability between providers, personas wanting different AI behaviors in different contexts, defaults for sensitive information categories, uncertainty communication about memories, preventing "AI psychosis" from distorted recollections, and enterprise memory governance balancing individual customization with organizational compliance.

## 5. The articulation barrier limits who can use AI effectively

Jakob Nielsen's concept of the **articulation barrier** identifies the gap between what users truly need and the extent to which they can articulate this need and make it explicit to AI. The barrier's significance becomes clear through literacy data: based on OECD's Program for International Assessment of Adult Competencies, approximately **50% of adults in the US and Germany qualify as "low-literacy users"**, rising to 85%+ in middle-income countries like Chile, Mexico, and Turkey. Since writing descriptive prose is more challenging than reading it, "low-articulation users" exceed low-literacy users. Nielsen predicts less than 20% of the population in advanced countries is sufficiently articulate to make advanced use of prompt-driven AI.

The existence of "prompt engineers" as a profession represents evidence of interface failureâ€”specialized professionals being needed to operate AI interfaces suggests these systems fail average users. Even literate domain experts rarely get software that meets actual needs when giving specifications to IT, as "decades of enterprise software development" demonstrates. Image generation requires vocabulary knowledge (cinematography terms like "boom shot" versus "pullback dolly," art styles, lighting terminology) that most users simply lack.

Six UX design patterns for prompt augmentation address this barrier with varying approaches. **Style galleries** display different creative directions users can select from rather than requiring vocabulary recall, leveraging Heuristic 6 "Recognition Rather than Recall." Freepik separates effects into color, camera, and lighting categories; Runway shows elaborate multi-style selections. **Prompt rewrite** lets AI enhance basic prompts with additional detailsâ€”Leonardo transforms "A robot looking puzzled" into 100+ word descriptions with artistic references. Users can then edit the enhanced prompt, since **editing is much easier than creation from scratch**. **Targeted prompt rewrite** specifies a direction for modification rather than generic enhancement, making only relevant changes when users request "add dramatic lighting."

**Related prompts** provide AI-suggested follow-ups based on initial queriesâ€”Perplexity's follow-up questions reportedly doubled user engagement after introduction. **Prompt builders** offer dropdown menus and structured templates for constructing prompts through selections rather than free text. Google Imagen's elaborate builder limits each dropdown to four choices for usability, similar to database query builders in enterprise software. **Parametrization** uses GUI elements like sliders and toggles to modify prompt dimensions without textual description. ChatGPT Canvas offers reading level sliders from Kindergarten to Graduate School. Critical design factor: parameters must be named in user-understandable terms, not internal technical namesâ€”ElevenLabs' "Stability" parameter requires users to discover through trial that it affects excitement level.

Magic prompts where AI writes prompts to feed itself have emerged as a distinct pattern. Ideogram offers three modes: Off (original prompt), On (enhance all generations), and Auto (AI decides based on prompt length). The feature adds "context, ambiance, and stylistic information." Leonardo's "Improve Your Prompt" and "Edit with AI" features transform simple descriptions into elaborate specifications via API-accessible endpoints. Shape of AI warns that elaborate prompts may create inflated expectations when results still disappoint, recommending showing generated prompts so users can "trace input to output."

Hybrid interfaces combining text and GUI represent Nielsen's best guess for successful AI interfaces: "successful AI user interfaces will be hybrid and combine elements of intent-based outcome specification and some aspects of the graphical user interface." GUIs show what's possible through recognition, text preserves flexibility for unique needs, and the combination reduces cognitive load for common operations. Writing assistants provide inline suggestions, toggleable modifiers offer checkboxes for common attributes, and visual plus textual input combinations like Kling's motion brush let users draw vectors indicating movement direction.

Voice mode offers promise for alleviating the articulation barrier, particularly for older users. Back-and-forth dialogue allows AI to piece together intent from multiple utterances. ChatGPT Advanced Voice Mode uses natively multimodal models that directly process audio rather than speech-to-text pipelines, capturing speaking pace and emotional tone with low-latency interruptible responses. User experience reviews describe "pressure in my chest suddenly gone" compared to previous voice modes requiring loud, clear speech. Current limitations include jumping in during pauses, lacking time concepts, and struggling to distinguish when two humans are conversing versus addressing the AI.

The tension between hiding complexity and building user capability remains unresolved. Hiding complexity serves users who "simply want to get their work done and log out," but creates "even wider gap between those who create/manage AI and those who utilize it" and risks atrophying essential communication skills. Nielsen's emerging thesis suggests moving from instruction to explorationâ€”"switch from users telling the computer what they want to users discovering what they need, through exploring a latent solutions space created by AI." The insight extends Recognition Rather than Recall from interface elements to outcomes themselves: "It is much easier to recognize that something fulfills your vision than it is to specify that vision."

## 6. Agent interfaces demand new visualization and approval paradigms

The paradigm shift from conversational to agentic AI creates interface challenges without precedent. Victor Dibia articulates the distinction: "Most 'agents' in production are mostly workflowsâ€”a set of deterministic steps designed by an engineer with a few LLM calls sprinkled in. Multi-Agent Systems where LLMs drive control flow of execution are suited to problem spaces where the exact solution is unknown." Key properties requiring UX consideration include autonomy (can do many different things), action (can take actions with side effects), and duration (complex long-running tasks).

Matt Biilmann's coinage of "Agent Experience (AX)" positions it as the next evolution after User Experience and Developer Experience. The insight reveals fundamental differences: **agents work differently from humans**â€”they read structured data rather than scanning pages visually, seek direct paths to information rather than clicking through interfaces, and find progressive disclosure (helpful for humans) to be an obstacle. Salesforce frames AX differently as designing AI agents for humans to use, where great AX leads to great UX because broken processes prevent agents from performing tasks. The definitional disagreement itself signals the field's immaturity.

OpenAI's Operator, launched January 2025, demonstrates trust-building through visual feedback loops showing real-time agent actions in contrast with voice-only interfaces. Users can take control from the agent at any point, with sandboxed remote browsing ensuring agents operate in isolated environments. Microsoft Copilot Studio agents use trigger-based activation responding to events like email arrival or scheduled times, with knowledge integration across Microsoft 365 Graph, Dataverse, and enterprise systems. Windows 11's agent integration creates a unified entry point combining Copilot, agent invocation, and search, with status indicators for working, needs approval, and completed states.

AutoGen Studio v0.4 exemplifies visualization approaches with drag-and-drop visual canvas for defining collaboration dynamics, real-time event-driven asynchronous messaging, and mid-execution controls for pausing conversations, redirecting actions, and adjusting team composition. Message flow visualization maps paths and dependencies. Google's Antigravity introduces the most developed "Mission Control" paradigm with an Agent Manager View dashboard for spawning, monitoring, and interacting with multiple agents. Parallel work stream visualization with status indicators shows artifacts produced and pending approval requests. Four tenets guide the design: Trust through artifacts and verification, Autonomy through access to editor/terminal/browser, Feedback through artifact comments, and Self-improvement through learning from past work.

Approval workflows have matured through frameworks like AWS's Agentic AI Security Scoping Matrix, which defines four scopes: Scope 1 with no agency and human-initiated actions, Scope 2 with prescribed agency requiring human approval for consequential actions, Scope 3 with supervised agency using policy-based thresholds, and Scope 4 with full agency and continuous behavioral validation. Dibia's cost-aware delegation principle advocates for specialized risk/cost classifiers assessing low/medium/high risk levels, with UX automatically allowing low-risk actions while requiring explicit approval for medium and high-risk operations.

LangGraph's interrupt() function enables pausing graph execution mid-trajectory, waiting for human input, then resuming with deterministic debuggable behavior. Confidence threshold routing configures agents with thresholds where below-threshold situations route automatically to human review queues. Temporal.io's durable execution pattern has emerged as infrastructure backbone, providing event-sourced history that stores not just states but every input, enabling crash recovery where **agents resume exactly where they left off from Event History**.

The supervisor/worker architecture dominates orchestration, with central orchestrators receiving requests, decomposing into subtasks, delegating to specialized workers, monitoring execution, and synthesizing responses. Visualization approaches vary: LangGraph uses graph-based representation with agents as nodes and connections as edges; AutoGen frames interactions as "conversation" between agents; Kore.ai uses three-layer visualization with Planner, Agents, and Tooling layers.

Convergences include universal human-in-the-loop requirements across all frameworks, near-universal adoption of graph-based visualization, shift from raw logs to artifact-based transparency, risk-based approval tiers with configurable thresholds, and durable execution as baseline expectation. Contradictions appear in conversation versus graph mental models, visibility spectrum decisions (showing every step versus hiding everything), and autonomy versus control tradeoffs.

Gaps persist in cost communication UX with no established patterns for effectively communicating token, API, and time costs. Multi-agent debugging when agents spawn sub-agents lacks mature hierarchical visualization. Cross-platform agent handoffs through MCP protocol remain UX-undefined. Long-running agent state for days or weeks of execution has no established progress representation. Confidence communication showing agent uncertainty has no established patterns for "I'm not sure" states.

## 7. Non-determinism fundamentally challenges software UX conventions

Non-determinism in LLMs represents a genuinely novel paradigm. Chatbots are deterministic with static, pre-designed responses; LLMs are notâ€”there's variability in outputs from identical inputs that breaks user expectations formed over decades of deterministic software. Recent academic research quantified the challenge: with LLMs configured to be deterministic (temperature=0), researchers observed **accuracy variations up to 15% across naturally occurring runs** with gaps between best and worst possible performance up to 70%. None of the tested LLMs consistently delivered repeatable accuracy across all tasks, much less identical output strings.

Temperature serves as the primary user-facing control, modifying softmax probability distributions. Low temperature (0-0.3) produces more deterministic, focused outputs ideal for factual tasks. High temperature (0.8+) enables more creative, diverse outputs suitable for brainstorming. Related parameters include Top-K limiting choices to the k most probable tokens and Top-P/nucleus sampling selecting from the smallest group meeting probability thresholds.

The Thinking Machines Lab breakthrough revealed that the common "concurrency + floating point" hypothesis is largely wrong. The real culprit is **batch variance**: when querying inference endpoints, server load is effectively non-deterministic from user perspective. Load determines batch size that kernels run under, changing results of individual requests. Standard GPU kernels for RMSNorm, matrix multiplication, and attention are not batch-invariantâ€”changing batch size changes reduction order, cascading into different outputs. Their experiment showed 1,000 identical prompts at temperature=0 yielding 80 unique completions in standard vLLM.

Mixture of Experts architectures introduce additional non-determinism through expert routing. Contents of each batch must be mapped to experts and returned as a batch, meaning competing inputs for the same experts may result in different expert assignments across batches. Load balancing mechanisms add noise to prevent expert collapse, and "token drop" can occur when experts reach capacity.

The desirability question reveals context-dependent answers. Creative applications benefit from controlled randomnessâ€”non-determinism provides effective ways to explore, higher temperatures encourage adventurous expressive output, and always selecting the most probable token leads to boring repetitive text. Critical applications demand determinism for scientific reproducibility (a bedrock of progress), debugging, legal or medical QA requiring audit trails, RL training (different numerics between training and inference implicitly turns on-policy RL into off-policy RL), and enterprise trust.

The Microsoft HAX Toolkit addresses expectation management through guidelines including making capabilities clear, communicating how often AI might make mistakes, engaging in disambiguation when uncertain, and enabling explanations. The Google PAIR Guidebook emphasizes that "people are more likely to have unachievable expectations for products that they assume have human-like capabilities" and the importance of communicating algorithmic nature and limits.

Exposure patterns vary by product category. Creative tools like Midjourney fully expose seed parameters for reproducibility (locking specific starting points across the 0-4294967295 range) while clearly communicating limitations like seeds not being portable across model versions. Production systems abstract away complexity, using lower temperatures and verification mechanisms. Hybrid approaches offer "creative" versus "precise" mode toggles, with "regenerate response" buttons implicitly acknowledging variability and positioning variation as feature rather than bug.

Reproducibility solutions have emerged from Thinking Machines Lab's batch-invariant kernels addressing three key operations: RMSNorm with fixed reduction order, matrix multiplication compiled for all shapes, and attention using "fixed split-size" strategy. Results show all 1,000 completions becoming identical versus 80 unique completions without the fix. Performance tradeoff is approximately 34-61% overhead. SGLang integrated this work for production use, maintaining compatibility with chunked prefill, CUDA graphs, and radix cache while enabling 100% reproducible RL training.

Gaps include the mental model question of how users form accurate models of probabilistic systems, expectation calibration given that choosing optimal temperature "is largely based on guesswork, gut instinct, non-systematic experimentation," cross-session consistency as seeds may produce different results across model versions or hardware, and MoE routing variability that is hard to eliminate due to load balancing requirements. No industry consensus exists on default temperature settings by use case, seed parameter naming, determinism guarantees in API contracts, or user-facing language for explaining variability. Regulatory implications for AI in regulated domains remain undefined.

## 8. Multi-modal integration requires rethinking modality coordination

Multi-modal AI represents a fundamental shift from single-input interfaces to systems integrating text, voice, visual, and gesture simultaneously. The core challenge identified in research: "What is the ideal interface for human-computer interaction with these powerful AI systems?" The chatbot model is inherently linear and struggles to seamlessly integrate multiple input types.

Modality transition patterns have evolved significantly. ChatGPT's November 2025 update addressed "modal lockout"â€”the friction where users had to stop reading to speakâ€”by integrating voice mode directly into chat windows. Real-time text transcription during voice interaction, visual elements appearing while speaking, and ability to interrupt AI's verbal summary to reference text elements enable seamless switching. Google Nest Hub's "Look and Talk" uses **eight machine learning models simultaneously** to detect intentional interaction through audio, video, and text analysis, detecting user intent via gaze direction, head orientation, and lip movement, allowing users within five feet to simply look at the screen and talk without wake words.

BMW's Panoramic iDrive demonstrates automotive multi-modal coordination through the "Act, Locate, Inform" principle distributing content across displays: Panoramic Vision for overall status, 3D Head-Up Display for turn-by-turn directions, central touchscreen for deeper exploration, and voice assistant via LLM for natural dialogue. The "My Modes" feature combines up to ten parameters activated with single voice commands, reconfiguring entire vehicles through phrases like "Efficient Mode" versus "Sport Mode."

Context retention across modalities presents persistent challenges. Research notes that "the GUI model often lacks context retention, making it difficult for the system to remember previous interactions." Apple Intelligence Siri maintains context across requests ("How are the Giants doing?" â†’ "When are they playing next?" â†’ "Add that to my calendar") and understands corrections mid-request. Google's Continued Conversation listens for eight seconds after responses for follow-ups, while Gemini Live allows resuming conversations up to one hour later.

Discoverability remains a leading reason users abandon voice assistants. Unlike GUIs with visible buttons and menus, voice and multi-modal interfaces lack visible affordances. Discovery patterns include onboarding tutorials, contextual cues and suggestions ("Try asking about your calendar today"), visual indicators (mic icons, animated feedback when listening), and platform-specific approaches like Google Nest Hub's "Discover" tab with command examples.

Error recovery patterns for modality conflicts include graceful degradation offering clarifying questions rather than failing silently, cross-modal confirmation showing visual confirmation when voice commands process, semantic alignment detection using cross-modality confusion matrices, and action-modality match principles that resolve conflicts when text and visual inputs disagree.

LLM-native multi-modal patterns differ fundamentally from legacy approaches. Legacy systems used command-and-control paradigms with fixed vocabularies, state-machine conversation flows, separate processing pipelines (Speechâ†’Textâ†’Processâ†’Textâ†’Speech), and limited context retention. LLM-native systems feature natural language understanding, context-aware conversation, multimodal fusion in single models (GPT-4o processes audio, vision, and text in single neural networks), native audio-to-audio processing without transcription cascades, and emotional nuance detection including tone, hesitation, and emphasis.

Gaps include temporal synchronization for multiple inputs arriving at different times, cross-modal consistency when visual and voice inputs conflict, context window limitations for multi-modal history, privacy concerns requiring always-listening hardware and camera access, and the discoverability problem where users remain uncertain what commands are possible. The cognitive load question of how many simultaneous modalities become overwhelming lacks clear answers. Trust building for AI that sees, hears, and understands presents tradeoffs between transparency and seamless experience. Industry-specific challenges include automotive safety implications of modality conflicts, smart home multi-user household complexities, and mobile battery and processing constraints for on-device AI.

## Cross-cutting observations and emerging research questions

Several patterns emerge across all eight thematic clusters that warrant synthesis beyond individual challenges. The **tension between transparency and usability** appears everywhereâ€”trust calibration benefits from showing reasoning but creates cognitive load; agency benefits from observability but overwhelms users; memory benefits from transparency but sacrifices "magical" experience. No universal resolution exists; context-dependent tradeoffs must be navigated deliberately rather than through defaults.

The **user capability versus system accommodation** debate manifests differently in each domain. Should prompting interfaces hide complexity or build user capability? Should memory systems automatically curate or require user curation? Should agents operate autonomously or require constant approval? The articulation barrier research suggests accommodation is necessary for majority adoption, yet building capability may create more resilient long-term users. This tension deserves explicit design consideration rather than implicit resolution through technical convenience.

**Expectation management** emerges as perhaps the most consistently underserved need. Users bring mental models from deterministic software, human relationships, and media depictions of AI. Technical reality diverges dramatically: stateless models with context injection, probabilistic outputs, limited memory windows, and imperfect reasoning. The gap between expectation and reality creates trust violations regardless of actual capability. Proactive expectation setting rather than reactive disappointment management should become a design priority.

The **convergence toward hybrid interfaces** appears across multiple domains. Prompt articulation benefits from combining text with GUI elements. Artifacts blend conversation with spatial persistence. Multi-modal integration combines voice with visual feedback. Agency combines autonomous action with human approval gates. The "AI-native" interface may not be pure conversation but rather sophisticated hybrids that leverage both natural language flexibility and graphical interface discoverability.

**Pattern library maturity varies dramatically** across challenges. Trust calibration has the most developed academic foundation with Amershi et al. and Zhang et al. providing validated guidelines. Agent UX is rapidly developing through Dibia's principles and framework proliferation. Memory patterns remain underdeveloped with competing approaches and no clear consensus. Non-determinism patterns are nearly absent despite the fundamental nature of the challenge.

Several **emerging research questions** deserve investigation across the field. How do users develop mental models of AI systems, and how can interfaces support accurate model formation? What is the optimal granularity for human-AI handoffsâ€”per-action, per-goal, per-session, or adaptive? How should AI systems signal uncertainty across different modalities and interaction contexts? What standards would enable interoperability across AI products for artifacts, memory, and agent protocols? How do cultural differences affect expectations and preferences across all eight domains?

The field has progressed remarkably in recognizing challenges, developing initial patterns, and shipping experiments. The next phase requires moving from pattern recognition to pattern validationâ€”systematic research on what actually works, for whom, and under what conditions. The practitioners currently inventing these interfaces in real-time deserve both the academic research foundation to ground their decisions and the pattern library resources to learn from each other's experiments. This synthesis represents one attempt to support that progress by mapping the current landscape of convergences, contradictions, and gaps across the fundamental challenges of designing interfaces for AI systems that are non-deterministic, increasingly autonomous, and fundamentally different from any software that came before.
