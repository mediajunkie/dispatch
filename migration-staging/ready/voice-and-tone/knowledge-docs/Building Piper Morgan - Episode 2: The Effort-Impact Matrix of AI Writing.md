# Building Piper Morgan - Episode 2: The Effort/Impact Matrix of AI Writing

You can't have two top priorities.

I learned this the hard way at 7 Cups when we were trying to simultaneously build a peer support platform AND a therapist marketplace AND a self-care app. Spoiler alert: we had zero top priorities, not three.

So when I started thinking about where AI writing assistance could actually help versus where it was just shiny-object syndrome, I did what any self-respecting product person would do: I made a 2x2 matrix.

## The Setup

Here's the thing about prioritization methods — they're never perfect, but they force you to think systematically about trade-offs. And with AI tools, the trade-offs are... weird.

Traditional effort/impact analysis assumes effort is about resources: time, money, engineering hours. With AI writing, effort is more like: How much context do I need to provide? How much editing will this require? How likely am I to spend more time fixing it than I would have spent writing it myself?

Impact is weird too. It's not just "will this move the needle on OKRs?" It's also: Will this free me up to do higher-value work? Will this help me maintain consistency across a massive documentation set? Will this let me actually ship on time?

(Yes, I just admitted that shipping on time is sometimes more important than perfection. Sue me.)

## Meanwhile, Back in the Real World...

Let me get concrete. Last week I had:
- A strategy doc that needed writing (high impact, genuinely needed my brain)
- Release notes for a feature update (medium impact, formulaic structure)
- Seventeen Slack messages explaining the same technical concept to different stakeholders (low impact individually, high effort cumulatively)
- A blog post I'd been meaning to write for three months (medium impact, but important for thought leadership)

Old me would have done them in order of deadline panic. New me ran them through the AI assistance matrix.

## The Matrix (No, Not That One)

**High Impact, Low Effort to Augment:**
- First drafts of routine communications
- Expanding bullet points into prose
- Reformatting existing content for different audiences
- Creating templates from examples

This is your sweet spot. Maximum leverage, minimum weirdness.

**High Impact, High Effort to Augment:**
- Complex technical documentation (needs tons of context)
- Strategic narratives (requires too much back-and-forth)
- Anything involving genuine creativity or novel insights
- Responses to sensitive situations

Here's where you need to be honest: is the effort to make AI helpful actually more than just doing it yourself?

**Low Impact, Low Effort to Augment:**
- Routine status updates
- Meeting summaries from notes
- Standard email responses
- Basic formatting tasks

Sure, use AI here. It's not changing the world, but every minute counts.

**Low Impact, High Effort to Augment:**
- Niche technical explanations that require extensive context
- Highly personalized messages
- Anything where your specific voice really matters

Just... don't. This is where AI assistance becomes AI theater.

## What I Actually Did

Remember that list from last week? Here's how it played out:

**Strategy doc:** Wrote it myself, but used AI to help structure my thoughts and create a comprehensive outline. (30% time savings, 100% still my thinking)

**Release notes:** Fed the AI my bullet points and a template from previous releases. Edited the output for accuracy. (70% time savings, good enough is good enough)

**Seventeen Slack explanations:** Created one good explanation with AI help, then customized it slightly for each audience. (80% time savings, plus bonus consistency)

**Blog post:** You're reading it. I wrote the opener and the framework, AI helped me flesh out the middle sections, I rewrote the conclusion. (50% time savings, and I actually shipped it)

## The Part Nobody Talks About

Here's what's actually hard about this: you have to be rigorous about tracking what works and what doesn't. It's tempting to use AI for everything (new toy syndrome) or nothing (luddite syndrome).

I started keeping a simple log:
- Task type
- Time estimated without AI
- Time actually spent (including AI wrangling)
- Quality assessment (did I have to rewrite it?)
- Would I do it again?

After three weeks, patterns emerged that surprised me. My assumptions about what would be easy for AI to help with were often backwards. Turns out it's great at helping me structure complex arguments but terrible at mimicking my sense of humor. (Its jokes are like dad jokes, but somehow worse?)

## The Leadership Angle

If you're thinking about rolling out AI writing tools for your team, here's my advice: start with the effort/impact matrix, but make it collaborative.

Have your team track their own experiments. What works for one person might be a disaster for another. Your principal engineer might find AI helpful for documentation but your UX writer might find it actively harmful for their work.

And please, for the love of all that is holy, don't mandate AI use for everything. That's like mandating that everyone use the same keyboard. Some people need mechanical switches, some people type on their phones, and some weirdos still use Dvorak layouts. (Hi, I see you.)

## Key Takeaways (Because Product Habits Die Hard)

1. **Not all writing tasks are created equal** — categorize before you delegate to AI
2. **Track your actual results** — assumptions about AI effectiveness are often wrong
3. **The sweet spot is augmentation, not replacement** — think bicycle, not car
4. **Different people will have different sweet spots** — what works for me might not work for you
5. **Good enough often IS good enough** — especially for routine communications
