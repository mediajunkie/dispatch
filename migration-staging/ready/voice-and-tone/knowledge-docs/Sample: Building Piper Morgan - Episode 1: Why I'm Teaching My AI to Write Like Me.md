# Building Piper Morgan - Episode 1: Why I'm Teaching My AI to Write Like Me

OK, so here's what I'm doing and why it might sound completely bananas at first: I'm training an AI to write in my voice. Not because I'm lazy (well, not *just* because I'm lazy), but because I think there's something genuinely interesting happening at the intersection of human expertise and AI capability that we're all dancing around but not quite talking about directly.

And before you @ me about "cheating" or whatever — stick with me here. This isn't about replacing human creativity. It's about something weirder and potentially more interesting.

## The Problem Nobody Wants to Admit

When the call came in to help with product documentation for a govtech project (yes, I'm back in the civic tech salt mines and loving it), I found myself staring at the same problem I've had for years: I can either write good documentation or ship good product, but rarely both at the same time.

Sound familiar?

We all know documentation matters. We all swear we'll keep it updated. We all fail spectacularly at this. It's like going to the gym — virtuous in theory, abandoned by February.

But here's the thing that changed: I realized I'd been thinking about AI writing assistance completely backward. I was treating it like a better spell-checker when I should have been treating it like... well, like I treat junior product managers.

## Meanwhile, Back in My Writing Cave...

I've written a book (*Product Management for UX People* — shameless plug, but hey, it's relevant), countless blog posts, documentation that actually got read (I know, right?), and enough Slack messages to fill a medium-sized library. 

My voice is all over the place. It's in git commits, in strategy docs, in rambling emails to colleagues at 2 AM when I suddenly figure out why that feature isn't working.

And I started thinking: What if I could teach an AI not just what I know, but *how I explain what I know*?

(Grammar wat? Yeah, I know. Stick with me.)

## The Experiment Begins

So I started feeding Claude (yes, I name my AI collaborators, fight me) examples of my writing. Not the polished stuff — the raw drafts, the blog posts I never published, the explanations I typed out in Slack when someone asked "but what exactly does a product manager do?"

You know what's wild? Patterns emerged that I didn't even know I had:
- I always start explanations with an accessible hook
- I use "OK, but..." to acknowledge and then reframe objections  
- I drop cultural references like breadcrumbs (apparently I can't go three paragraphs without mentioning the Dead)
- I explain technical concepts using physical metaphors
- My parenthetical asides have parenthetical asides (like this (seriously))

## The "Cheating" Question

Let's address the elephant in the room. Is this cheating? Is this the death of authentic writing? Am I just another tech bro trying to automate away human creativity?

*Deep breath*

No. And here's why.

When I taught junior PMs at 7 Cups, I didn't expect them to reinvent product management from first principles. I gave them templates, frameworks, examples of good specs. I showed them how I think through problems. I edited their work to sound more like "proper" product documentation.

How is this different?

The AI isn't creating ideas from nothing. It's taking my ideas, my expertise, my weird tendency to compare everything to either sailing or jazz improvisation, and helping me express them in my voice when I need to be in three places at once.

## What Actually Works (And What Spectacularly Doesn't)

Here's what I've learned so far:

**Works:**
- Blog posts and documentation (shocking, I know)
- First drafts of routine communications
- Expanding bullet points into actual prose
- Maintaining consistent voice across long documents

**Doesn't Work:**
- Anything requiring genuine creativity or novel insights
- Responses to nuanced interpersonal situations
- Technical architecture decisions (duh)
- Humor (it tries, bless its heart, but... no)

**Weird Middle Ground:**
- It can extend metaphors I start but can't create new ones that feel authentic
- It nails my structural patterns but sometimes misses emotional undertones
- It's *too good* at my parenthetical style (I've had to dial it back)

## The Part Where I Get Philosophical

There's something profound here about tools and augmentation. When I was a kid learning guitar, I used a pick. Was that cheating? When I started using IDEs with autocomplete for coding, was that cheating? When designers moved from paper to Figma, was that—

You get the point.

The question isn't whether we should use these tools. The question is how we use them thoughtfully, ethically, and in ways that amplify rather than replace human judgment and creativity.

## Key Takeaways (Because Old Habits Die Hard)

1. **AI writing assistance is like having a really good intern who's read everything you've ever written** — useful, but needs supervision
2. **Your voice is more consistent than you think** — patterns emerge when you analyze enough of your writing
3. **The ethical questions are real and worth wrestling with** — but maybe not in the binary way we typically frame them
4. **This is about augmentation, not replacement** — I still need to know what's worth saying

## What's Next

I'm going to keep documenting this experiment. Partly because I think it's genuinely useful for other folks wrestling with similar questions. Partly because I'm fascinated by what happens when you teach a machine to be a better version of your own communication style. And partly because, well, I committed to writing more this year and this seems like a great way to eat my own dogfood.

Next up: I'll share the actual voice guide I created and walk through how I tested and refined it. (Spoiler: it involved a lot of "that sounds like me but I would never actually say that" moments.)

Meanwhile, back at the actual work that pays my bills, I'm using this to help document a complex technical architecture for a system that helps veterans access benefits. The stakes are real, the need for clear communication is critical, and I literally cannot write fast enough to keep up with what needs documenting.

Is it perfect? Hell no. 
Is it helping? Absolutely.
Is it weird? Very.

Welcome to the weird future, friends. Let's figure it out together.
