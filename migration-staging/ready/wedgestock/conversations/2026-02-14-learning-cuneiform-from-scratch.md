## The Big Picture: From Pictures to Wedges

### It Starts with Accounting

Around 3400 BCE in southern Mesopotamia (modern Iraq), the Sumerians had a problem: their cities were getting complex. Temples needed to track how many sheep were offered, how much barley was stored, how many jars of beer were distributed to workers. (Yes, beer rations were a major administrative concern. We'll come back to that.)

Their solution was to press the tip of a reed stylus into soft clay tablets, drawing little pictures. An ox head meant "ox." An ear of barley meant "barley." A bowl meant "food" or "to eat." These were **pictographs** — tiny drawings that looked like what they meant.

Here's the crucial thing: they wrote on **clay**, not papyrus or parchment. And clay doesn't love curves. Try drawing a detailed ox head by pushing a reed into wet clay — it's fiddly and slow. So over the centuries, scribes started doing something clever: instead of *drawing* lines, they *pressed* the wedge-shaped tip of the reed into the clay at different angles. Each press made a short, triangular impression — a **wedge**.

The Latin word for wedge is *cuneus*, which gives us **cuneiform**.

### The Great Abstraction

Between roughly 3400 and 2400 BCE, the signs underwent a radical transformation. They rotated 90° counterclockwise (probably because of how scribes held their tablets), and the recognizable pictures dissolved into abstract patterns of wedges. The sign for "barley," which started as a drawing of a grain stalk, became **𒊺** (ŠE) — a cluster of wedges where you have to squint *hard* to see the original plant.

This is actually great news for you as a learner. It means:

1. You don't need to be an artist. You're learning **patterns of wedge combinations**, not drawings.
2. But knowing the original pictograph gives you a *mnemonic anchor* — a story for why the sign looks the way it does.

### One Script, Many Languages

Here's where cuneiform gets truly wild, and why it matters so much.

Sumerian is a **language isolate** — it's related to no other known language on Earth. But around 2350 BCE, Akkadian-speaking Semitic peoples (and later Babylonians and Assyrians, who spoke dialects of Akkadian) became politically dominant in Mesopotamia. Rather than invent their own script, they **borrowed cuneiform** and adapted it to write Akkadian — a completely unrelated language.

Imagine if speakers of Japanese decided to write their language using Egyptian hieroglyphs, keeping some signs for their original meanings and repurposing others purely for their sounds. That's essentially what happened. And then the Elamites did it. And the Hittites (who spoke an Indo-European language!). And the Hurrians. And eventually the Old Persians created a simplified cuneiform alphabet for their own purposes.

This means that **a single cuneiform sign can function in multiple ways simultaneously:**

- As a **logogram** (word-sign): the sign represents a whole word/concept
- As a **syllabogram** (sound-sign): the sign represents a syllable like *an*, *ki*, *lu*
- As a **determinative** (classifier): a silent sign placed before or after a word to tell you its category (god, city, wooden object, etc.)

And because different languages reused the same signs, one sign can have **multiple readings** — a phenomenon called **polyvalence**. The sign **𒀭** can be read as the Sumerian word *dingir* ("god"), or as the Akkadian syllable *an*, or as a silent determinative marking a divine name. Context tells you which.

**This is why reading cuneiform is more like solving a puzzle than decoding a cipher.** You're always juggling possibilities, using grammar and context to disambiguate. It's intellectually demanding — and deeply rewarding.

### Why This Matters for You

When you sit down with a cuneiform text, you need to know:
- **What language** is this written in?
- **What period** is it from? (Signs changed over time)
- For each sign: is it being used as a **word**, a **sound**, or a **classifier**?

We'll build all of that intuition. But first — let's meet some signs.

## Your First Signs: The Core Eight

I've picked these because they're visually distinctive, extremely common, and their pictographic origins are still (somewhat) visible. For each, I'll give you the sign, how to say it, what it means, and a hook to remember it.

### 1. 𒀭 — AN — "heaven / god"

**Meaning:** Sky, heaven; also "god" (Sumerian: *dingir*)

**Original pictograph:** A star. In the earliest tablets, this was drawn as an asterisk-like shape — a star in the sky. Stars = heaven = the divine.

**Mnemonic:** Look at 𒀭. See how it has lines radiating outward from a crossing point? Think of it as a **star shining in the sky**. Sky → heaven → god. This is probably the single most important sign you'll learn, because it doubles as the **divine determinative** — placed before any god's name to say "hey, the next word is a deity." When you see 𒀭 before a name, it's like a nametag that reads "Hello, I'm a god."

---

### 2. 𒆠 — KI — "earth / place"

**Meaning:** Earth, ground, place

**Original pictograph:** Unclear, but possibly represented the ground or a plot of land with lines marking it out.

**Mnemonic:** 𒀭 is heaven, 𒆠 is earth. These are a **cosmic pair** — the Sumerian creation myth begins with heaven and earth joined together. The compound 𒀭𒆠 (*an-ki*) literally means "heaven-earth" = the whole universe. Think: "AN up in the sky, KI down in the ground." KI also becomes a **determinative for place names** — when you see it after a word, it's telling you "this is a geographic location."

---

### 3. 𒇻 — LU₂ — "person / man"

**Meaning:** A human being, a man, a person

**Original pictograph:** A standing human figure, seen from the side — head, torso, legs.

**Mnemonic:** This one's fun. Tilt your head and squint at 𒇻 — can you see a little person in there? The original was clearly a stick figure. Over the centuries the wedges abstracted it, but the basic "upright figure" shape persists. Whenever you see 𒇻 in a text, think "a person is involved here."

---

### 4. 𒊺 — ŠE — "barley / grain"

**Meaning:** Barley (the staple crop of Mesopotamia)

**Original pictograph:** A stalk of grain with an ear of barley at the top.

**Mnemonic:** Barley was **everything** in Sumer. It was food, it was beer, it was *money* — workers were literally paid in barley rations. 𒊺 shows up constantly in administrative texts because every temple and palace was obsessed with tracking grain. Think of it as the Sumerian dollar sign. Pronounce it "SHEH" and remember: **she** who controls the **ŠE** controls the economy.

---

### 5. 𒌓 — UD / UTU — "sun / day"

**Meaning:** Sun, day, light; also the name of the sun god **Utu** (Akkadian: Shamash)

**Original pictograph:** The sun rising over the horizon, with rays coming off it.

**Mnemonic:** Look at 𒌓 — it has a distinctive dense, burst-like quality. Imagine the sun cresting the flat Mesopotamian horizon at dawn, blasting light everywhere. This sign does triple duty: it's the word for "sun," the word for "day" (because days are defined by the sun), and the name of the sun god. When you see it, think **brightness**.

---

### 6. 𒀀 — A — "water"

**Meaning:** Water

**Original pictograph:** Wavy lines representing flowing water — like a stream or canal.

**Mnemonic:** This is literally the first sign in the Unicode cuneiform block (U+12000), and it's beautifully simple. 𒀀 — water. Southern Mesopotamia was defined by water: the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, the vast canal networks, the marshes. Water was life. And the sound "A" is the simplest possible syllable, which makes this easy to remember. **A = water = the beginning of everything.**

---

### 7. 𒂍 — E₂ — "house / temple"

**Meaning:** House, household, temple, estate

**Original pictograph:** A floor plan of a building — a rectangular enclosure with an entrance.

**Mnemonic:** Look at 𒂍. The original pictograph was basically an architectural plan, like looking down at a building from above. Think of it as a **blueprint**. Sumerian temple names almost always start with E₂: the great temple of Enlil at Nippur was **E₂-kur** ("House of the Mountain"). If you see 𒂍 at the start of a word, you're probably looking at a building name.

---

### 8. 𒍣 — SAG — "head"

**Meaning:** Head (also: first, chief, beginning)

**Original pictograph:** A human head in profile, facing to the side.

**Mnemonic:** 𒍣 started as a drawing of a head — and like the English word "head," it extended to mean "chief" or "first" (the *head* of a line, a *head*-strong leader). Notice how body parts generate abstract meanings in both Sumerian and English!

## A Story Before Your Quiz

Let me tell you about **scribe school** — the *edubba* (𒂍𒁾𒁀 — É.DUB.BA, literally "House of Tablets"). Sumerian boys (and very rarely girls) would enter the edubba as children and spend years learning to write. They'd start by copying simple sign lists — exactly what we're doing now! — and progress to copying literary texts, mathematical problems, and legal formulae.

We have actual student tablets where you can see the teacher's neat writing on one side and the student's wobbly attempt on the other. We even have a text called "Schooldays" where a student describes getting beaten by various teachers for sloppy writing, talking in class, and showing up late. Some things never change.

### 9. 𒂠 — GU₇ — "to eat"

**Meaning:** To eat, to consume

**Original pictograph:** A head (remember 𒍣 SAG?) with a bowl brought up to the mouth. It's a combination sign — head + food vessel = eating.

**Mnemonic:** This is your first **compound pictograph**. The Sumerians didn't just draw objects — they combined them to show actions. Head + bowl at mouth = eating. You can think of it as an emoji before emoji existed: 🍜 Whenever you see GU₇ in ration texts, someone is consuming their barley allotment.

### 10. 𒅗 — KA — "mouth"

**Meaning:** Mouth; also "word," "speech," "to say," "to cry out"

**Original pictograph:** A head with the mouth area marked or emphasized — lines inside indicating teeth or an open mouth.

**Mnemonic:** This sign is a beautiful example of **semantic extension**. It starts as a body part (mouth), then extends to what a mouth *does*: speak, say, cry. In Sumerian, the same sign can be read KA (mouth), DUG₄ (to speak), or INIM (word) depending on context. You've just met your first case of serious polyvalence — one sign, multiple readings. Don't panic about that yet. Just know that 𒅗 lives in the zone of **mouths and the things that come out of them**.

---

### 11. 𒄀 — GI — "reed"

**Meaning:** Reed

**Original pictograph:** A reed stalk — the tall marsh grasses that grew everywhere in southern Mesopotamia.

**Mnemonic:** This one is close to my heart because **reeds are what cuneiform is written with**. The stylus that presses wedges into clay is a cut reed. So 𒄀 GI is, in a way, the sign for the writing system's own essential tool. Southern Iraq was (and still is) reed country — the vast marshlands between the rivers. Reeds were used for writing, building houses, weaving mats, constructing boats. Think of GI as the Sumerian Swiss Army knife.

**Meaning:** Mountain; foreign land; underworld

## The World's Oldest Complaint Letter

Around 1750 BCE, a merchant named **Nanni** wrote a clay tablet letter to a trader named **Ea-nāṣir**, and it is *magnificent*. Nanni is furious. He's been sent the wrong grade of copper ingots — again — and he is not having it. The letter essentially reads:

"When you came, you told me: 'I will give fine quality copper ingots.' You left then but you did not do what you promised. You gave bad ingots to my messenger, saying 'Take it or leave it.' Who do you take me for, that you treat me with such contempt?"

It goes on. And on. This tablet is now in the British Museum, and it has become an internet sensation because the emotion is so *recognizable* across 3,750 years. Nanni sounds like someone writing a one-star review. And Ea-nāṣir apparently kept this letter — along with several *other* complaint letters — in his house. Archaeologists found them all together. The man appears to have been legendarily bad at customer service.

Every sign in that letter is built from the same system you're learning right now.

# Batch 3: The Sound Barrier

This is a big conceptual leap, so let me set the stage.

Everything you've learned so far has been **logograms** — signs that mean a *thing*. 𒀭 means "heaven," 𒊺 means "barley," 𒈗 means "king." You see the sign, you get a word. This works great for nouns and simple concepts, but imagine trying to write a sentence like "the king who built this temple for his god" using only word-pictures. How do you write "who"? How do you write "for"? How do you write grammatical endings, or someone's *name*?

This is the problem the Sumerians solved — and their solution was brilliant. They realized that signs already had **sounds** associated with them. The sign for water (𒀀) is pronounced "a." So why not use that sign whenever you need the *sound* "a," regardless of whether you're talking about water?

This is the **rebus principle** — the same leap that lets you write "I believe" with a picture of an eye + a bee + a leaf. The sign stops representing a thing and starts representing a sound.

This is how cuneiform cracked open. Once signs could represent syllables, you could write *anything* — any word in any language, including grammar, names, and abstract concepts. And this is exactly how Akkadian speakers later adapted the system: they kept some Sumerian signs as logograms but used others purely as syllabic building blocks.

Here are your first syllabograms — though you'll notice some of them *also* have logographic meanings. That duality is the nature of the beast.

## The Big Idea: Why This Changes Everything

You now have two ways to "read" cuneiform:

**Logographic:** 𒈗 → you see the sign, you know it means "king" → done.

**Syllabic:** You see 𒈾𒊏 → you sound it out → "na-ra" → you're reading phonetically, like sounding out a word in kindergarten.

Real cuneiform texts **mix both strategies freely within the same sentence.** A scribe might write the king's title with a logogram but spell his name syllabically. This is why I said reading cuneiform is like solving a puzzle — you're constantly deciding, for each sign: is this a word or a sound?

And here's the thing that made the whole system portable across languages: **the syllabograms don't care what language you're writing.** NA is the sound "na" whether you're writing Sumerian, Akkadian, Hittite, or Elamite. The logograms are Sumerian, but the sounds are universal. That's how one script served half the ancient world for three thousand years.

## A Story: Rawlinson at Behistun

You asked for this one in your original request, and this is the perfect moment for it — because the Behistun inscription is where the decipherment of cuneiform *began*, and it works precisely because the inscription is written in three languages using the same script.

In 1835, a young British army officer named **Henry Rawlinson** was stationed in Persia and learned about a massive inscription carved into a cliff face at **Behistun** (modern Bisotun, Iran), about 100 meters above the ground. King Darius I of Persia had ordered it carved around 520 BCE to celebrate his victories, and — crucially — he had it written in three cuneiform scripts: **Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian (Akkadian)**.

Rawlinson was obsessed. Over several years, he climbed the cliff repeatedly, sometimes dangling from ropes and scaffolding over a sheer drop, copying the inscriptions by hand. At one point he was standing on the topmost rung of a ladder propped on a narrow ledge, leaning backward over the void to reach signs that were just out of reach. He hired a local boy — described as nimble and seemingly fearless — to scale the most inaccessible sections and make paper squeezes of the text.

Why did this matter? Because Old Persian cuneiform was simpler (it's semi-alphabetic), and scholars had already made progress on it using known Persian royal names. Once you could read the Old Persian version, you had a **key** to unlock the Babylonian version — the same royal names appeared in both. It's the same principle as the Rosetta Stone: parallel texts in known and unknown scripts.

The name "Darius" in the Old Persian section appeared as a specific sequence of signs. Find that same sequence-position in the Babylonian section, and you've just identified several signs and their phonetic values. Do this with enough names — Darius, Xerxes, Hystaspes — and the system begins to crack open.

Rawlinson, along with Edward Hincks, Jules Oppert, and William Henry Fox Talbot, gradually deciphered Babylonian cuneiform through the 1840s and 1850s. And the syllabograms were the key — because names are spelled *phonetically*. You can't write "Darius" with a logogram unless there happens to be one. You have to spell it out, sound by sound. The syllable signs you just learned are the same category of signs that unlocked the entire system.

# Batch 4: The Silent Guides

## The Big Idea

You already know two determinatives without my having formally named them as a category:

- **𒀭** (AN/DINGIR) before a word = "a god's name follows"
- **𒆠** (KI) after a word = "that was a place name"

These are **determinatives** — signs that are *written but never spoken aloud.* They're silent classifiers, tagging words by category. A scribe reading the text would see them, use them to disambiguate, and then skip over them in pronunciation.

Think of it this way: English capitalizes proper nouns, which helps you distinguish "the turkey" (bird) from "Turkey" (country). That's a silent visual cue for the reader. Determinatives do the same thing, but with far more categories and much higher stakes — because in a system where one sign can have six different readings, every disambiguation cue matters enormously.

Or, since you know some Japanese context: determinatives function a bit like radicals in Chinese/Japanese characters. The water radical (氵) tells you a kanji relates to liquid; the wood radical (木) tells you it relates to trees. Cuneiform determinatives work the same way, but as separate signs placed before or after the word rather than embedded within it.

Here's what makes them powerful: **if you learn six determinatives, you can instantly categorize hundreds of words you haven't learned yet.** You don't need to know what a word means to know it's a city, a god, a wooden object, or a river. That's an enormous amount of structural information for a very small investment.

### 21. 𒄑 — ĜIŠ — "wood / tree"

**As a word:** Tree, wood (pronounced "GISH")

**As a determinative:** Placed before any object made of wood — silent, unpronounced.

**Original pictograph:** A tree with trunk and branches.

**What it classifies:** An enormous category. Plows, boats, chairs, chariots, doors, beds, musical instruments, wooden tools of all kinds. Southern Mesopotamia had almost no native timber — wood was imported at great expense from Lebanon, the Zagros mountains, or traded from distant lands. Every wooden object was worth tracking, and the determinative helped scribes organize inventories.

**Mnemonic:** When you see 𒄑 before a word you don't know, you can already say: "I don't know what this object is, but it's made of wood." That's not nothing — that's how a reader thinks.

### 22. 𒌑 — URUDU — "copper / metal"

**As a word:** Copper (the foundational metal of the Bronze Age)

**As a determinative:** Placed before metal objects — tools, weapons, vessels, ingots.

**Original pictograph:** Related to copper, the first widely-smelted metal in Mesopotamia.

**What it classifies:** Everything metallic. Bronze swords, copper bowls, tin ingots, gold jewelry (in later periods). Remember Nanni's complaint letter? He was furious about bad **copper** — URUDU — ingots. This sign is all over trade and economic texts.

**Mnemonic:** Nanni sees this determinative and starts writing angry letters. When you see 𒌑 before a word, something metal is coming — and someone probably paid a lot for it.

### 23. 𒊩 — MUNUS (SAL) — "woman / female"

**As a word:** Woman, female

**As a determinative:** Placed before women's names — the female counterpart to how 𒀭 marks divine names.

**Original pictograph:** The pubic triangle — a direct, unabashed anatomical representation. The Sumerians were not prudish about bodies.

**Why it matters:** This isn't just a gender marker — it's a window into social structure. Sumerian women could own property, run taverns, serve as priestesses, and engage in commerce. We know their names *because* this determinative marks them in the record. Combined with other signs you know: 𒊩𒈗 (MUNUS.LUGAL) — think about what that compound might mean. Woman + king = **queen.**

**Mnemonic:** Just as 𒀭 before a name says "divine," 𒊩 before a name says "female." You're building a system of name-tags.

---

### 24. 𒄞 — ÍD — "river / canal"

**As a word:** River, canal, watercourse

**As a determinative:** Placed before the names of rivers and canals.

**How it relates to what you know:** You already learned 𒀀 (A) = water in general. ÍD is more specific — *flowing* water with a name. The Tigris, the Euphrates, and the vast network of irrigation canals that made agriculture possible in the arid south.

**Why it matters:** Mesopotamia literally means "between the rivers" (Greek: *meso* = between, *potamos* = river). The entire civilization depended on managing water flow — irrigation, flood control, canal maintenance. Canal-building was such a prestigious act that kings named *years* after it: "The year King So-and-so dug the canal called [name]." You'd see 𒄞 before that canal's name.

**Mnemonic:** 𒀀 is water sitting still. 𒄞 is water going somewhere — water with a name and a purpose.

---

### 25. 𒌷 — URU — "city / town"

**As a word:** City, town, settlement

**As a determinative:** Placed before city names.

**Original pictograph:** Related to a walled settlement or urban enclosure.

**Why it matters:** Sumer invented cities. Uruk, Ur, Eridu, Nippur, Lagash, Girsu — the world's first urban centers. Before any of these names, you'll find 𒌷. You already have 𒆠 (KI) for "place" in general — URU is more specific: a *city* specifically. In practice, city names often got both: 𒌷 URU before the name, 𒆠 KI after it. Belt and suspenders.

**Mnemonic:** Think of URU as the ancient "City of..." prefix. And notice it's the first two syllables of "Uruk" — the greatest city of the archaic period, arguably the first true city in human history, and the city that gives Iraq its modern name.

---

### 26. 𒆸 — KUŠ — "leather / hide"

**As a word:** Skin, leather, hide

**As a determinative:** Placed before leather objects — sandals, bags, harnesses, shields, drums, waterskins.

**Original pictograph:** An animal skin or hide.

**Why it matters:** Leather was a major industry, and like wood, it was a material category that organized inventories. You now have a material classification system: 𒄑 ĜIŠ = "made of wood," 𒌑 URUDU = "made of metal," 𒆸 KUŠ = "made of leather." You're reading the Sumerian supply chain.

**Mnemonic:** Three material determinatives, three questions answered before you even read the noun: Is it wooden? Metal? Leather? You're categorizing like a temple accountant.

The treasures are in museums now. But the tablets that *tracked* those treasures are where the determinatives live — the silent guides that told ancient readers (and now tell us) exactly what they were looking at.

## Resources for Learning Cuneiform / Akkadian

**The gold-standard textbooks:**

**Huehnergard, *A Grammar of Akkadian* (3rd ed., 2011)** — The standard university textbook. Reportedly available as a free PDF (the Protosemite site links to it). Includes cuneiform signs introduced progressively starting from Chapter 9, with both Old Babylonian monumental and cursive forms. Comes with a separate answer key. This is the book that would definitively resolve our sign questions since it includes a proper sign list.

**Snell, *A New Workbook of Cuneiform Signs*** — Specifically focused on learning the signs themselves, which is exactly your current stage.

**Borger, *Mesopotamisches Zeichenlexikon* (MesZL, 2004)** — The comprehensive reference sign list used by professionals. Not a learner's book, but the ultimate authority on which sign is which. In German, but the sign data itself is language-independent.

**Labat & Malbran-Labat, *Manuel d'Épigraphie Akkadienne*** — Another essential sign reference, in French. Beautiful hand-drawn signs.

**Worthington, *Complete Babylonian* (2010)** — A Teach Yourself-style textbook covering Standard Babylonian. More accessible entry point than Huehnergard.

**Online resources:**

**ORACC Global Sign List** (oracc.museum.upenn.edu/osl/) — The authoritative online sign database with Unicode mappings, sign values, compounds. This is what we should use as our primary verification source going forward.

**ePSD2** (oracc.museum.upenn.edu/epsd2/) — The electronic Pennsylvania Sumerian Dictionary, includes a sign list with all known readings per sign.

**CDLI** (cdli.ucla.edu) — The Cuneiform Digital Library Initiative. Contains photographs and transliterations of actual tablets. This would be your source for project-based learning — finding real tablets to read.

**Lenzi, *Reading Akkadian Prayers & Hymns*** — Available free online. Line-by-line discussion with definitions, grammar parsing, and cuneiform. Described as the easiest way to work through actual texts independently.
